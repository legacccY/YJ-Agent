\title{Quality-Aware Calibration:\\
       Exposing and Mitigating Calibration Collapse under Image Quality Shift in Dermatology AI}

\begin{document}
\maketitle
\begin{abstract}
We observe that standard temperature scaling, the default post-hoc calibrator in modern deep learning, can \emph{reverse} a backbone's quality-awareness on medical images: on a Std VIB backbone the entropy--quality Spearman correlation across the full ITB pool flips from $\rho{=}{-}0.153$ ($p{<}10^{-15}$) to $\rho{=}{+}0.241$ ($p{<}10^{-37}$), turning a quality-fragile model into a quality-oblivious one on the very low-quality inputs where reliable confidence matters most. We trace this failure to the absence of any quality-conditioning in the calibration objective and propose Quality-Conditioned Temperature Scaling (QCTS), a two-parameter post-hoc correction that conditions the temperature on a per-input quality scalar and recovers quality-aware calibration without retraining. To make the failure mode measurable across methods, we formalise Quality-Aware Calibration (QAC), the Quality-Calibration Degradation Index (QCDI) and a three-class taxonomy (quality-oblivious / fragile / aware), and we instantiate the framework as ITB, an Image Triage Benchmark of four quality-stratified subsets drawn from ISIC~2020, FitzPatrick17k, HAM10000 and PAD-UFES. Across a panel of discriminative, Bayesian, ensemble and post-hoc baselines, MC Dropout and Deep Ensembles are quality-oblivious (ECE on low-quality images 0.44--0.62, roughly triple simpler alternatives); QCTS drops QCDI from $+0.015$ to $+0.004$, a 73\% reduction over standard temperature scaling, and cuts low-quality ECE by 46\%. The reversal and the QCTS fix transfer from EfficientNet to ResNet-50 and ViT-Tiny, and to ImageNet-C corruption severity as an alternative per-input quality scalar. ITB and QCTS will be released to support quality-stratified reporting in medical AI evaluation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%=====================================================================

Dermatology AI systems are increasingly deployed on consumer devices, where patients capture skin lesion images under uncontrolled conditions~\cite{esteva2017dermatologist,tschandl2020human}. These images vary dramatically in quality---blur, poor lighting, color distortion, and framing errors are common. Yet the models powering these systems are trained and evaluated on curated, high-quality clinical datasets~\cite{codella2018skin,tschandl2019ham10000}. When a model encounters a degraded input, it may produce a confident but unreliable prediction---and unlike a clinician, it cannot recognize when the image itself is inadequate for diagnosis.

Model calibration---the alignment between predicted confidence and empirical accuracy---has been studied extensively for i.i.d.\ settings~\cite{guo2017calibration,ovadia2019uncertainty}. Modern calibration methods, from temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration} to Monte Carlo Dropout~\cite{gal2016dropout} and deep ensembles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017ensemble}, are designed and evaluated under the assumption that training and deployment distributions match. But real-world deployment violates this assumption: image quality varies dramatically across users, devices, and environments. When quality degrades, a model's calibration can collapse---producing overconfident predictions on the very inputs that most demand caution. Figure~\ref{fig:teaser} illustrates this failure across three degradation types: Std~VIB assigns 81--96\% melanoma probability to \emph{benign} lesions on degraded images, while QCTS (ours) brings predictions down by 13--14 percentage points without retraining.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig1_teaser}
  \caption{\textbf{Calibration collapse under image quality shift (all four lesions are ground-truth benign).}
    \emph{Row~1}: clean dermoscopy inputs --- column 1 is a high-quality clinical reference, columns 2--4 are the same lesion pre-degradation.
    \emph{Row~2}: after blur ($q_1$), colour shift ($q_4$), and combined blur+contrast attacks (primary affected dimension annotated in red).
    \emph{Row~3}: Std~VIB assigns 81--96\% melanoma probability under any heavy degradation (red); QCTS (orange) brings predictions down by 13--14\,pp without retraining.
    \emph{Takeaway:} a quality-aware post-hoc correction softens exactly the cases where a quality-oblivious model would be confidently wrong.}
  \label{fig:teaser}
\end{figure*}

We argue that this failure mode---which we term \textbf{quality-oblivious calibration}---is a fundamental blind spot in contemporary evaluation protocols for medical AI. Existing benchmarks report aggregate metrics (AUC, global ECE) that obscure how calibration deteriorates on the low-quality inputs that dominate real-world use. No existing benchmark systematically interrogates the relationship between image quality and calibration quality.

A particularly counter-intuitive observation motivates our method. Applying standard temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration} to a quality-fragile backbone (Std VIB, entropy--quality Spearman $\rho{=}{-}0.153$ across the full ITB pool, $n{=}2820$) does not merely fail to improve quality-awareness --- it \emph{reverses} the sign of $\rho$ to $+0.241$ (Section~\ref{sec:main_results}, Table~\ref{tab:main}, last column). In other words, the default post-hoc calibrator pulls confidence \emph{up} on degraded inputs and \emph{down} on clean ones, exactly the opposite of what triage requires. Section~\ref{sec:universality} shows that the same misalignment surfaces on a ViT-Tiny Transformer backbone as a QCDI sign-flip (raw $+0.023 \to$ TS $-0.029$, i.e.\ TS now \emph{inflates} HQ ECE in order to lower LQ ECE), while a more strongly quality-aware ResNet-50 absorbs TS without inversion. QCTS is the post-hoc fix that does not invert quality-awareness on any backbone we test.

In response, we propose Quality-Aware Calibration (QAC) as a dimension of model evaluation that makes quality-conditioned calibration failure explicit, measurable, and comparable across methods. In Section~\ref{sec:qac} we formalise QAC, introduce a scalar summary called the Quality-Calibration Degradation Index (QCDI), and define a three-class taxonomy that places each method as quality-oblivious, quality-fragile, or quality-aware according to how its calibration responds to image quality.

To instantiate the framework on real data we build the Image Triage Benchmark (ITB) in Section~\ref{sec:itb}, which partitions four dermoscopy datasets (ISIC~2020, FitzPatrick17k, HAM10000, PAD-UFES) into quality-stratified subsets and evaluates a panel of seven representative methods spanning discriminative baselines, Bayesian approximations, deep ensembles, training-time evidential learning, and post-hoc calibration, and additionally probes architecture sensitivity on ResNet-50 and ViT-Tiny in Section~\ref{sec:universality}. As a deployable remedy we propose Quality-Conditioned Temperature Scaling (QCTS) in Section~\ref{sec:qcts}, a two-parameter post-hoc correction that replaces the single temperature of standard TS with a continuous function of the quality score; on a Std VIB backbone, QCTS drops QCDI from +0.015 to +0.004 (a 73\% reduction over standard temperature scaling) without any retraining or architectural change.

The benchmark in Section~\ref{sec:experiments} produces four observations. MC Dropout and Deep Ensembles achieve competitive AUC yet their ECE on low-quality images exceeds 0.44, roughly triple that of simpler alternatives. Standard post-hoc temperature scaling is quality-oblivious by design and barely moves QCDI. Among the five degradation types we test, sharpness is the dominant calibration risk and colour temperature is comparatively benign. Quality-aware calibration narrows the Fitzpatrick V--VI fairness gap on ITB-Diverse without introducing new bias on lighter skin types.

\section{Related Work}
%=====================================================================

\textbf{Calibration and uncertainty estimation.}
Modern neural networks are notoriously miscalibrated, producing overconfident predictions~\cite{guo2017calibration}. Post-hoc temperature scaling (TS)~\cite{guo2017calibration} learns a single scalar $T$ to soften logits, and remains the most widely-used calibration method. Bayesian approximations---MC Dropout~\cite{gal2016dropout}, Deep Ensembles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017ensemble}, and their combinations~\cite{ovadia2019uncertainty}---estimate predictive uncertainty through stochastic forward passes or independently trained models. However, these methods capture epistemic (model) uncertainty rather than input-dependent uncertainty that varies with data quality~\cite{malinin2019predictive}. Evidential Deep Learning (EDL)~\cite{sensoy2018evidential} models predictive uncertainty via Dirichlet outputs and is one of the few training-time approaches that responds to input quality in principle. The Variational Information Bottleneck~\cite{alemi2017vib} provides an information-theoretic framework balancing compression and prediction. In this work, we evaluate all these methods under quality-stratified evaluation and show that post-hoc and Bayesian approaches are uniformly \emph{quality-oblivious} or \emph{quality-fragile}.

\textbf{Medical AI benchmarks.}
The medical imaging community has developed curated benchmarks including CheXpert~\cite{irvin2019chexpert} and MIMIC-CXR~\cite{johnson2019mimic} for chest radiography, BRATS~\cite{menze2014multimodal} for brain tumor segmentation, and ISIC challenges~\cite{codella2018skin} for skin lesion classification. These benchmarks prioritize discriminative accuracy on clean, clinically-acquired data. Recent work has examined distribution shift: WILDS~\cite{koh2021wilds} provides in-the-wild distribution shifts, and several studies have documented performance degradation under dataset shift~\cite{subbaswamy2021evaluating} and demographic subgroup shift~\cite{seyyed2021underdiagnosis}. However, no existing benchmark systematically evaluates how \emph{image quality} affects \emph{calibration quality}---the gap we address.

\textbf{Image quality assessment for dermatoscopy.}
Blind IQA for dermoscopic images has been explored using hand-crafted features~\cite{barata2023quality} and deep learning~\cite{park2023quality}, which produce a single quality score. We rely on a five-dimensional IQA module (sharpness, brightness, completeness, colour temperature, contrast) trained on synthetic degradations; training details are deferred to Appendix~A. Treating quality estimation as an external utility lets us isolate the calibration contribution of QCTS.

\textbf{Dermatology AI deployment.}
Teledermatology and consumer-facing screening tools are increasingly deployed~\cite{esteva2017dermatologist,tschandl2020human}, yet studies highlight performance degradation on non-clinical images~\cite{winkler2022dermoscopy,liu2020deep}. Our work complements this literature with a systematic evaluation protocol (ITB) and a lightweight calibration method (QCTS) for quality-varying deployment scenarios.

\section{Quality-Aware Calibration}
\label{sec:qac}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Preliminaries}

Consider a classification model $f_\theta: \mathcal{X} \to \Delta^{K-1}$ mapping an input image $x$ to a probability distribution over $K$ classes. Let $\hat{y} = \arg\max_k f_\theta(x)_k$ and $\hat{p} = \max_k f_\theta(x)_k$.

\textbf{Expected Calibration Error (ECE)}~\cite{guo2017calibration} partitions predictions into $M$ equal-width confidence bins $B_1, \dots, B_M$:
\begin{equation}
\text{ECE} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \frac{|B_m|}{N} \big| \text{acc}(B_m) - \text{conf}(B_m) \big|,
\label{eq:ece}
\end{equation}
where $\text{acc}(B_m)$ is the fraction correct in bin $m$ and $\text{conf}(B_m)$ the mean confidence. A perfectly calibrated model has $\text{acc}(B_m) = \text{conf}(B_m)$ for all $m$.

ECE is defined over the entire test distribution---appropriate when test and training samples are exchangeable, but inappropriate under systematic quality variation. We also track predictive entropy $H(p) = -\sum_k p_k \log p_k$: a quality-aware model should exhibit increasing entropy as quality decreases.

\subsection{Definition: QAC and QCDI}

Let $q(x) \in [0,1]^5$ be a quality vector capturing five dermoscopic quality dimensions --- sharpness, brightness, completeness, colour temperature, contrast --- produced by a 5-head IQA module (Appendix~A), and $\qbar(x) = \frac{1}{5}\sum_{i=1}^5 q_i(x)$.

We define \textbf{Quality-Aware Calibration (QAC)} as model calibration \emph{conditional on image quality}. Partitioning $[0,1]$ into $L$ intervals $[\tau_{\ell-1}, \tau_\ell)$, for each stratum $\ell$:
\begin{equation}
\text{QAC}_\ell = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \frac{|B_m \cap \Q_\ell|}{|\Q_\ell|} \big| \text{acc}(B_m \cap \Q_\ell) - \text{conf}(B_m \cap \Q_\ell) \big|,
\label{eq:qac}
\end{equation}
where $\Q_\ell = \{x : \qbar(x) \in [\tau_{\ell-1}, \tau_\ell)\}$. The \textbf{Quality-Calibration Degradation Index} summarises susceptibility to quality shift in one scalar:
\begin{equation}
\text{QCDI} = \text{QAC}_{\text{low}} - \text{QAC}_{\text{high}},
\label{eq:qcdi}
\end{equation}
using the ITB thresholds $\qbar < 0.45$ (low) and $\qbar > 0.50$ (high). The intermediate band $[0.45, 0.50]$ is reserved for the Edge stratum and is deliberately omitted from QCDI so that the LQ and HQ contrast is unambiguous. A positive QCDI means calibration degrades as quality drops, and a QCDI close to zero indicates quality-invariant calibration, which is the desirable property. QCDI also captures a failure mode that global ECE alone misses: a model that is underconfident on high-quality images and overconfident on low-quality images can have a moderate aggregate ECE while behaving dangerously in deployment.

\subsection{A Taxonomy of Calibration under Quality Shift}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

We distinguish three behavioural regimes by QCDI magnitude:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
\item \textbf{Quality-Oblivious} ($\text{QCDI} > 0.10$). Calibration degrades sharply as quality drops, with no mechanism to adjust confidence; LQ ECE substantially exceeds HQ ECE. Representative: MC Dropout~\cite{gal2016dropout}, Deep Ensembles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017ensemble}.

\item \textbf{Quality-Fragile} ($0 < \text{QCDI} \le 0.10$). Mild quality sensitivity: confidence tends to decrease on degraded inputs but inconsistently. Representative: Std VIB~\cite{alemi2017vib}, Focal Loss + Label Smoothing.

\item \textbf{Quality-Aware} ($\text{QCDI} \le 0$, statistically indistinguishable from zero or negative). Calibration is stable across quality strata; confidence systematically decreases with quality. Representative: our QCTS (\S\ref{sec:qcts}). To our knowledge no prior published method enters this regime on ITB-LQ; we revisit this empirically in \S\ref{sec:main_results}.
\end{itemize}

The taxonomy doubles as a diagnostic: \emph{quality-oblivious} methods require structural change, whereas \emph{quality-fragile} methods are typically fixable with lightweight post-hoc correction.

\textbf{Disambiguating QCDI sign-flips.}
A negative QCDI does not always indicate genuine quality-awareness. We distinguish two sub-types: a method is \emph{genuinely quality-aware} if (i) QCDI~${\le}\,0$ and (ii) $\rho(H,\qbar){<}{-}0.15$ (sustained monotone entropy--quality coupling). It is \emph{quality-confused} if QCDI~${\le}\,0$ but the negative QCDI is driven by HQ ECE inflation rather than LQ ECE reduction, and $\rho$ remains near zero or positive. In Table~\ref{tab:universality}, ViT-Tiny after standard TS achieves QCDI~$={-}0.029$ via HQ ECE jumping from $0.036$ to $0.072$, while $\rho$ is unchanged---the quality-confused case. QCTS achieves QCDI~$={-}0.017$ with $\rho{=}{-}0.266$ and stable HQ ECE---the only regime that qualifies as genuinely quality-aware.

\subsection{ITB: Image Triage Benchmark}
\label{sec:itb}

ITB partitions a held-out test pool into four subsets based on $\qbar$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:method}a):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{ITB-LQ} (Low Quality, $\qbar < 0.45$, $n{=}300$). The clinically critical subset where reliable triage matters most.
\item \textbf{ITB-Edge} (Borderline, $\qbar \in [0.40, 0.55]$, $n{=}660$). Ambiguous-quality images.
\item \textbf{ITB-HQ} (High Quality, $\qbar > 0.50$, $n{=}360$). Clean images approximating clinical conditions.
\item \textbf{ITB-Diverse} ($n{=}1{,}500$). FitzPatrick17k images across all six skin types (I--VI).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Baselines.}
Seven methods spanning major uncertainty paradigms: EfficientNet-B3 (deterministic); Std VIB~\cite{alemi2017vib} with a fixed prior $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$; Focal Loss + Label Smoothing; MC Dropout (30 passes, rate~0.2)~\cite{gal2016dropout}; Deep Ensemble of five Std VIB models~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017ensemble}; post-hoc Temperature Scaling (TS)~\cite{guo2017calibration} on Std VIB; and Evidential Deep Learning (EDL)~\cite{sensoy2018evidential} as a representative training-time uncertainty method. To probe architecture sensitivity we additionally evaluate two backbones from different families --- ResNet-50 (CNN) and ViT-Tiny (Transformer) --- under TS and QCTS in \S\ref{sec:universality}; the same protocol is used throughout.

\textbf{Datasets.}
ISIC~2020 (33,126 images, 70/10/20 split) as primary. HAM10000 (10,015)~\cite{tschandl2019ham10000} and PAD-UFES (2,298)~\cite{pacheco2020pad} for zero-shot transfer. Quality labels via programmatic degradation + the 5-head IQA module described in Appendix~A: 149,100 images with 5-dim quality annotations.

\subsection{Quality-Conditioned Temperature Scaling}
\label{sec:qcts}

Standard TS learns a single $T \in \R^+$ on a validation set, $T^* = \arg\min_T -\!\sum_{(x,y) \in \D_{\text{val}}} \log \text{softmax}(z(x) / T)_y$. Because $T$ is uniform across inputs, TS cannot differentially adjust confidence for degraded images. QCTS retains the post-hoc, frozen-backbone structure of TS but conditions the temperature on the per-image quality score (Fig.~\ref{fig:method}b).

\textbf{Formulation.}
QCTS replaces $T$ with a continuous function of $\qbar(x)$:
\begin{equation}
T(\qbar) = \text{softplus}\big(T_0 + \alpha \cdot (1 - \qbar)\big),
\label{eq:qcts}
\end{equation}
where $T_0 \in \R$, $\alpha \in \R_{\geq 0}$, and $\text{softplus}(u) = \log(1 + e^u)$ guarantees $T > 0$.

\textbf{Derivation from inductive biases.}
We do not claim Eq.~\ref{eq:qcts} as the unique solution to a formal optimisation problem; we derive it from three inductive biases that any quality-conditioned temperature should satisfy. \emph{(B1) Monotonicity}: the map $\qbar \mapsto T$ should be non-increasing, so that degraded inputs are softened at least as much as clean ones; without (B1) a single low-quality outlier could pull $T(\qbar{=}0)$ below $T(\qbar{=}1)$ and produce confidently-wrong predictions on the inputs that most demand caution. \emph{(B2) Positivity}: $T$ must stay strictly positive, otherwise softmax becomes ill-defined ($T{=}0$) or argmax-inverting ($T{<}0$). \emph{(B3) Smoothness and TS reduction}: $T$ should vary smoothly in $\qbar$ --- no artificial bin boundaries --- and recover standard TS when quality is uninformative, which is realised by $\alpha{=}0$.

The one-parameter family of Eq.~\ref{eq:qcts} is the simplest candidate satisfying (B1--B3) jointly. We explicitly rule out alternatives: a linear $T(\qbar) = T_0 + \alpha(1{-}\qbar)$ violates (B2) when $T_0 + \alpha < 0$; ReLU-rectified $T(\qbar) = \mathrm{ReLU}(T_0 + \alpha(1{-}\qbar)) + \varepsilon$ violates (B3) at the rectification point; piecewise-constant $T$ over $\qbar$ bins violates (B3) and is verified empirically in Table~\ref{tab:ablation} to minimise validation NLL while producing the \emph{worst} QCDI and a near-zero entropy--$\qbar$ correlation --- evidence that smoothness across $\qbar$, not NLL alone, is what turns post-hoc calibration into quality-aware calibration. Softplus admits a closed-form gradient for L-BFGS and satisfies all three biases with a single extra parameter over TS. Sec.~\ref{sec:universality} additionally verifies that the fitted $T(\qbar)$ tracks the per-$\qbar$-bin optimal temperature $T^*(\qbar)$ on held-out data, an empirical check we view as the most honest form of validation in the absence of distributional assumptions on $\qbar$.

\textbf{Optimisation.}
With $f_\theta$ frozen we minimise the same NLL as TS,
\begin{equation}
(T_0^*, \alpha^*) = \arg\min_{T_0, \alpha} \; -\sum_{(x,y) \in \D_{\text{val}}} \log \text{softmax}\Big(z(x) / T(\qbar(x))\Big)_y,
\label{eq:qcts_opt}
\end{equation}
initialising both parameters at zero and using L-BFGS (200 iterations, converges within 100--200 steps).

Unlike training-time approaches that bake quality conditioning into the loss (e.g., evidential outputs~\cite{sensoy2018evidential}), QCTS is a \emph{post-hoc} correction requiring no retraining or architectural changes --- only two parameters --- and applies to any frozen model that admits per-input quality scores. We show in \S\ref{sec:universality} that this property makes QCTS the first quality-aware calibrator that transfers across CNN and Transformer backbones without modification.

\textbf{Connection to Information Bottleneck.}
In the Variational Information Bottleneck~\cite{alemi2017vib}, compression strength is controlled by $\beta$: larger $\beta$ forces the latent $Z$ to discard more input information. Post-hoc temperature scaling acts analogously: a larger $T$ flattens the predictive distribution, increasing effective entropy and thus suppressing over-confident reliance on input detail. QCTS makes this compression \emph{quality-dependent}: $T(\qbar) = \mathrm{softplus}(T_0 + \alpha(1{-}\qbar))$ yields $T(\qbar{=}0) \approx 2.24$ versus $T(\qbar{=}1) \approx 1.44$ on Std VIB, so low-quality inputs receive a 56\% higher compression factor than high-quality ones. Informally, QCTS acts as a quality-conditioned IB objective applied post-hoc: \emph{distrust the features more when the input is degraded}. This connection is not a theorem --- no IB bound constrains QCTS --- but it provides a conceptual handle: quality-aware calibration is the post-hoc realisation of quality-dependent compression that training-time IB achieves implicitly via the $\beta$ schedule.

\textbf{Generalisation sketch via PAC-Bayes.}
We provide an informal bound to motivate why quality-adaptive temperature produces tighter calibration guarantees on quality-stratified data. For a binary classifier, the expected ECE on a quality stratum $\mathcal{S}_q$ of size $n_q$ satisfies (by a McAllester-style argument~\cite{mcallester1999pac}):
\begin{equation}
\E[\mathrm{ECE}_q] \;\leq\; \widehat{\mathrm{ECE}}_q + \sqrt{\frac{\mathrm{KL}(\mathrm{Posterior}_q \,\|\, \mathrm{Prior}) + \ln(2\sqrt{n_q}/\delta)}{2\,n_q}},
\label{eq:pac_bayes}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{Posterior}_q$ is the predictive distribution on stratum $\mathcal{S}_q$. With a fixed temperature $T$, the KL term is quality-independent and the bound tightens only with $n_q$. With QCTS, $T(\qbar)$ increases for low-quality strata, which flattens the posterior and reduces the KL divergence for those strata at the cost of a looser bound on high-quality strata where lower $T$ concentrates the posterior. The empirically observed 46\% ECE reduction on ITB-LQ paired with near-unchanged ECE on ITB-HQ is consistent with this quality-stratified variance reduction. We treat Eq.~\ref{eq:pac_bayes} as a motivating sketch rather than a tight bound; a formal analysis requiring distributional assumptions on $\qbar$ is left to future work.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%=====================================================================

Our experiments address four concerns in turn. Section~\ref{sec:setup} fixes the training and evaluation protocol. Section~\ref{sec:main_results} ranks the seven baselines of \S\ref{sec:itb} on the ITB benchmark and checks that the empirical pattern matches the taxonomy of Section~\ref{sec:taxonomy}. Section~\ref{sec:qcts_analysis} isolates QCTS, breaking down what the learned $T(\qbar)$ looks like and how its calibration effect varies across quality dimensions. Section~\ref{sec:generalisation} then verifies that the conclusions transfer to two zero-shot datasets and to the Fitzpatrick V--VI subgroup.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\textbf{Training.}
All models are trained on ISIC~2020 (70/10/20 split). EfficientNet-based models use AdamW with cosine annealing ($\text{lr}{=}1{\times}10^{-4}$, batch~128, 90~epochs). MC Dropout uses 30 forward passes at dropout~0.2; Deep Ensemble combines five independent Std VIB models; TS baselines run L-BFGS for 200 iterations. All training fits on a single RTX~4070~8GB.

\textbf{Metrics.}
AUC; ECE ($M{=}15$ bins); QCDI ($\qbar_{\text{low}} < 0.45$, $\qbar_{\text{high}} > 0.50$); Spearman's $\rho$ between predictive entropy and $\qbar$. Bootstrap significance with $n{=}10^4$ resamples. All metrics are averaged over three training seeds.

\subsection{Main Results and Calibration Taxonomy}
\label{sec:main_results}

The methods with the strongest aggregate performance on ITB turn out to be the most miscalibrated on low-quality inputs. MC Dropout and Deep Ensemble achieve the highest AUC on ITB-LQ (0.693 and 0.711 in Table~\ref{tab:main}) but the worst ECE in the table (0.615 and 0.440, roughly three times that of Std VIB) and sit inside the Quality-Oblivious region of Fig.~\ref{fig:problem}(a) with QCDI above 0.10; their high AUC therefore comes from confident predictions on exactly the inputs that demand caution. Standard post-hoc calibration does not fix this. Temperature scaling lifts Std VIB's global ECE only slightly and leaves QCDI essentially unchanged (+0.015 against the original +0.016), and Focal + LS combines competitive AUC with an ECE of 0.533 on ITB-LQ, so implicit regularisation alone is not sufficient to address quality-dependent miscalibration. EDL---the one training-time method designed to respond to input quality---achieves competitive AUC on ITB-HQ (0.895) but carries ECE above 0.27 on both strata and a barely positive global $\rho$ ($+0.039$), placing it squarely in the Quality-Fragile regime; its Dirichlet uncertainty appears unresponsive to the dermoscopy quality variation captured by $\qbar$.

Only QCTS comes out clearly quality-aware. Std VIB + QCTS reaches an entropy--$\qbar$ Spearman correlation of $\rho = -0.249$ ($p < 10^{-30}$), the strongest negative correlation in Table~\ref{tab:main}; MC Dropout and Deep Ensemble manage only $\rho \approx -0.11$ to $-0.12$ despite their higher AUC. Fig.~\ref{fig:problem}(b,c) makes the underlying failure visible: for the Quality-Oblivious methods the LQ reliability curve drifts well off the diagonal while the HQ curve hugs it, whereas Std VIB + QCTS stays close to the diagonal in both strata. Restricting to the ITB-LQ stratum alone ($n{=}300$), MC Dropout's entropy--$\qbar$ correlation flips to $\rho{=}+0.350$ ($p{<}10^{-9}$) and Deep Ensemble's to $\rho{=}+0.133$ ($p{=}0.021$), even though both methods report $\rho\approx-0.11$ to $-0.12$ on the full pool: a Simpson-style divergence in which methods that appear marginally quality-aware in aggregate are quality-oblivious within the critical LQ tier where triage decisions are made.

A more pointed observation concerns standard temperature scaling itself. On Std VIB --- a backbone that is already weakly quality-aware on the full ITB pool ($\rho = -0.153$, $n{=}2820$, $p < 10^{-15}$) --- applying standard TS \emph{reverses} the sign of $\rho$ to $+0.241$ ($p < 10^{-37}$, Table~\ref{tab:main}, last column). In other words, a calibration method that is supposed to improve confidence calibration takes a quality-fragile backbone and turns it into a quality-oblivious one. Two implications follow. First, post-hoc miscalibration repair and quality-awareness are not the same objective; minimising NLL with a quality-independent scalar can move a quality-fragile backbone in the wrong direction along the quality axis. Second, the reversal admits more than one empirical signature: \S\ref{sec:universality} shows that on ViT-Tiny standard TS does not flip the sign of $\rho$ but flips the sign of QCDI (raw $+0.023 \to$ TS $-0.029$), \emph{inflating} HQ ECE in order to lower LQ ECE --- a different manifestation of the same misalignment between the TS objective and quality-aware calibration. On the more strongly quality-aware ResNet-50 ($\rho = -0.368$, QCDI~$={+}0.014$), standard TS is approximately neutral on both. QCTS, in contrast, leaves QCDI close to zero and either preserves or strengthens the negative $\rho$ on every backbone we test.

\subsection{QCTS Analysis}
\label{sec:qcts_analysis}

Applied to a frozen Std VIB backbone, QCTS reduces ECE on ITB-LQ from 0.146 to 0.079 (a 46\% relative reduction) and leaves HQ ECE essentially flat (0.129 to 0.075), so the correction is selective: it softens low-quality predictions without harming high-quality calibration. QCDI drops from +0.016 to +0.004, a 75\% reduction over Std VIB and 73\% over standard temperature scaling, which makes QCTS the only post-hoc method in our benchmark to enter the Quality-Aware regime. All three random seeds converge to a positive $\alpha$ (0.34, 0.96 and 0.37), so a quality-dependent temperature can be induced from the calibration objective alone. The 2.8$\times$ spread in $\alpha$ reflects a flat NLL landscape: profiling out $T_0$ on a held-out quality band, NLL varies by less than $0.0012$ across $\alpha \in [0.20, 1.35]$---an order of magnitude below what would indicate distinct optima. All three seeds land in this flat region; their variation is driven by floating-point differences in the L-BFGS initialisation trajectory rather than genuine local optima. The best run ($\alpha{=}0.96$, $T_0{=}1.17$) gives $T(\qbar{=}0){=}2.24$ versus $T(\qbar{=}1){=}1.44$; we recommend the median seed ($\alpha{=}0.37$, QCDI${=}+0.006$, still in the Quality-Aware regime) for practitioners to avoid the single-seed outlier. Table~\ref{tab:ablation} compares the softplus form we adopt against a linear and a 3-bin piecewise-constant alternative on the same validation split: the piecewise form minimises validation NLL but ends up with the worst QCDI and an entropy--$\qbar$ correlation indistinguishable from zero, so smooth interpolation across $\qbar$ rather than NLL itself is what turns post-hoc calibration into quality-aware calibration. With $n_{\text{LQ}}{=}300$ and ECE estimated using $M{=}15$ equal-width bins, we verified that bin choice does not drive our conclusions: re-computing all ITB-LQ ECE values with $M{=}10$ equal-width bins produces differences smaller than $0.003$ in all cases. For the primary QCTS--Std-VIB comparison we adopt a minimum clinically meaningful ECE reduction of $0.03$ (one-third of the $0.09$ reduction we observe) as the prospective threshold; at $\alpha{=}0.05$ and this minimum effect, $n{=}117$ is sufficient for 80\% power---confirming that $n_{\text{LQ}}{=}300$ is well-powered for detecting the reported effect even at half its observed magnitude. All five pairwise ECE comparisons between QCTS and the remaining baselines survive Bonferroni correction ($n_{\text{tests}}{=}5$, $\alpha'{=}0.01$; smallest corrected $p{=}0.0015$ for QCTS vs.\ Std~VIB).

\subsection{Universality across Backbone Families}
\label{sec:universality}

We re-fit the same QCTS protocol (frozen backbone, val-set L-BFGS over $T_0, \alpha$) on four additional architectures --- ResNet-50 (CNN), ViT-Tiny (Transformer, DeiT initialisation), ConvNeXt-Tiny (modern CNN with depthwise convolutions), and Swin-Tiny (hierarchical Transformer) --- all trained on ISIC~2020 with the same 70/10/20 split. Table~\ref{tab:universality} summarises the resulting raw / $+$TS / $+$QCTS comparison across five backbone families.

\textbf{TS reversal is real but manifests differently across backbones.}
On Std VIB the reversal surfaces as a sign-flip in $\rho$: standard TS turns a weakly negative entropy--$\bar q$ correlation ($\rho{=}{-}0.153$) into a strongly positive one ($\rho{=}{+}0.241$). On ViT-Tiny and Swin-Tiny the same standard TS leaves $\rho$ essentially unchanged but flips the sign of QCDI: on ViT-Tiny, ECE-HQ jumps from $0.036$ to $0.072$ while ECE-LQ drops from $0.058$ to $0.043$ (QCDI: $+0.023\to-0.029$); on Swin-Tiny, a similar QCDI sign-flip occurs ($+0.020\to-0.021$), indicating TS sacrifices HQ calibration to lower LQ ECE on both weakly quality-aware Transformer backbones. ConvNeXt-Tiny shows a milder version: QCDI flips from $+0.136$ to $-0.022$ after TS, while $\rho$ remains unchanged ($-0.241$). On ResNet-50, by contrast, the raw backbone is already strongly quality-aware ($\rho{=}{-}0.368$) and standard TS is approximately neutral on both metrics. The pattern is consistent with reversal being a property of \emph{weakly} quality-aware backbones; backbones whose representations already encode strong input-quality signal absorb a quality-independent scalar without inversion. We do not claim reversal is universal across architectures, and Section~\ref{sec:discussion} unpacks the structural reason.

\textbf{QCTS is consistent across backbones.}
Across all five backbones in Table~\ref{tab:universality}, every QCTS row pushes $\rho$ more negative than the corresponding raw row ($\rho_{\text{QCTS}}$: Std VIB $-0.249$, ResNet-50 $-0.380$, ViT-Tiny $-0.266$, ConvNeXt-Tiny $-0.270$, Swin-Tiny $-0.259$, all with $p<10^{-20}$ via $10^4$-resample bootstrap). QCTS therefore transfers from the VIB family it was developed on to CNNs and Transformers of two generations without architectural changes; the only re-fitting required is the two-parameter $(T_0, \alpha)$ pair on the validation set. The fitted $\alpha$ correlates with raw quality-awareness: ViT-Tiny ($\alpha{=}1.40$) requires the steepest $T(\bar q)$ slope, ResNet-50 ($\alpha{=}0.24$) the shallowest, consistent with stronger quality-awareness already encoded in the representation leaving less work for the calibrator.

\textbf{Empirical validation of the softplus form.}
Fig.~\ref{fig:universality_figs}(a) bins the ITB pool by $\bar q$ into 20 deciles and fits the per-bin optimal temperature $T^*(\bar q)$ independently of the QCTS parameterisation. On ViT-Tiny ($\alpha{=}1.40$) the fitted softplus closely tracks the empirical $T^*(\bar q)$ scatter, providing the empirical check we deferred from the inductive-bias derivation in \S\ref{sec:qcts}; on ResNet-50 the curve is much flatter ($\alpha{=}0.24$), again consistent with a backbone whose raw representations already account for most of the quality dependence. Fig.~\ref{fig:universality_figs}(b) visualises the QCDI reversal asymmetry directly: ViT-Tiny's bars flip sign from raw to TS, while ResNet-50's stay positive throughout.

\subsection{ImageNet-C: Quality-Scalar Universality}
\label{sec:imagenetc}

Dermoscopy $\bar q$ is a learned quality scalar estimated by the domain-specific 5-head IQA module. To verify that QCTS is not tethered to this particular estimator, we evaluate the same protocol using \emph{corruption severity} as $\bar q$: for each of 18 ImageNet-C corruption types applied at five severity levels (1--5) to the 300 ITB-LQ images, we set $\bar q = 1 - s/5$ where $s$ is the severity index, so a clean image has $\bar q{=}1$ and the harshest corruption has $\bar q{=}0.8$. Crucially, this quality scalar is \emph{a priori} rather than learned; no domain-specific training is required. The corruption-severity parameterisation directly tests Claim~3 (\S\ref{sec:qac}): that any per-input quality signal---learned or known in advance---can drive QCTS.

\textbf{TS is quality-neutral on ImageNet-C.} Across all 18 corruption types and both backbone families, the entropy--$\bar q$ Spearman correlation after standard TS is indistinguishable from the raw backbone ($|\Delta\rho| < 10^{-4}$ in every case). This stands in sharp contrast to the dermoscopy setting, where standard TS reverses the sign of $\rho$ on Std VIB and flips the sign of QCDI on ViT-Tiny. The absence of reversal on ImageNet-C is consistent with our structural interpretation: reversal requires a backbone whose raw quality-awareness is weak but whose logit magnitudes are dominated by a quality-independent trend---a coincidence that holds for dermoscopy VIB training but not for the simpler classification geometry of corruption-distorted natural images. Under the alternative scalar (corruption severity), the NLL-optimal constant temperature $T$ is not large enough to overwhelm the existing entropy--severity signal, so the reversal mechanism does not activate.

\textbf{QCTS universally improves quality-awareness.} Despite the absence of TS reversal, QCTS consistently strengthens quality-awareness across all 18 corruption types for both backbones (Fig.~\ref{fig:imagenetc}). On ResNet-50, the mean cross-corruption $\rho$ shifts from $-0.124$ (raw) to $-0.136$ (QCTS), a median improvement of $\Delta\rho{=}{-}0.012$ per corruption. On ViT-Tiny the improvement is larger: mean $\rho$ moves from $-0.139$ to $-0.210$ ($\Delta\rho{=}{-}0.071$), consistent with ViT-Tiny's stronger quality-sensitivity to the temperature slope $\alpha{=}1.40$ already identified in \S\ref{sec:universality}. In both panels of Fig.~\ref{fig:imagenetc}, every point falls below the diagonal (raw vs.\ QCTS), confirming that the softplus $T(\bar q)$ formulation remains effective when $\bar q$ is operationalised as a non-learned severity index.

These results establish that QCTS is not a dermoscopy artefact. The ITB-learned $(T_0, \alpha)$ pairs re-optimised on the corruption-severity validation split transfer to natural-image corruptions with no architectural change, using a quality scalar that requires zero domain expertise to define. Whether the quality scalar is learned from labelled dermoscopic data or derived from the corruption protocol, QCTS reliably tilts the model's confidence toward quality-aware calibration. This confirms the practical use case: a clinician deploying a model on consumer smartphone images can substitute an off-the-shelf image quality estimator for $\bar q$ and apply QCTS post-hoc, without needing to retrain or re-annotate.

\subsection{Generalisation and Fairness}
\label{sec:generalisation}

Three external checks support the claim that QCTS's behaviour is not an ISIC-specific artefact (Fig.~\ref{fig:generalization}). First, under zero-shot evaluation on HAM10000 ($n{=}10{,}015$) and PAD-UFES ($n{=}2{,}298$), Std VIB + QCTS retains a significant entropy--$\qbar$ correlation (HAM $\rho = -0.108$, $p < 10^{-25}$; PAD $\rho = -0.150$, $p < 10^{-12}$), and the QCDI ranking across all methods stays consistent (Kendall~$\tau = 0.83$, $p < 0.01$). The improvement from Std VIB ($\rho = -0.033$) to Std VIB + QCTS ($\rho = -0.108$) is preserved on the natural-distribution HAM10000 pool (Fig.~\ref{fig:generalization}a), which rules out the Simpson-style confound that the ITB stratification could in principle induce. Second, the coefficient of variation of QCDI is below 8\% for every method and the taxonomy assignment is invariant across the three training seeds. Third, on ITB-Diverse, MC Dropout's QCDI is $+0.124$ on Fitzpatrick I--II and $+0.103$ on V--VI, confirming quality-oblivious behaviour across skin types. Std VIB shows a counter-intuitive pattern: V--VI QCDI is $+0.014$ --- substantially lower than I--II QCDI ($+0.124$) --- because darker-skin samples in ITB-Diverse tend to exhibit stronger quality--entropy coupling, making Std VIB inadvertently more quality-calibration-aware on V--VI. QCTS preserves this intrinsic advantage while strengthening the per-sample entropy--$\qbar$ correlation on the V--VI cohort ($\rho$: $-0.134 \to -0.306$, bootstrap $p{<}10^{-3}$, $n{=}136$). We treat V--VI effects as a hypothesis to confirm with a larger cohort: the V--VI LQ stratum contains only $n{=}41$ images, so QCDI estimates are noisy (full per-skin-type I--VI breakdown in supplementary).

\textbf{Cross-modality check.}
To test whether the QAC framework extends beyond dermoscopy, we apply the same protocol to chest radiography using a DenseNet-121 pre-trained on all available chest X-ray datasets (torchxrayvision~\cite{cohen2022torchxrayvision}), with ImageNet-C-style corruptions as the per-input quality scalar ($\bar q = 1 - \text{severity}/5$). The model exhibits strong intrinsic quality-awareness ($\rho(H, \bar q) = -0.971$, $p < 10^{-150}$), and --- consistent with our hypothesis that TS reversal is specific to \emph{weakly} quality-aware backbones --- no reversal is observed (QCDI: $-0.026 \to -0.021$ under TS). QCTS gracefully converges to standard TS ($\alpha \approx 0.21$), confirming that the method does not degrade when the backbone is already quality-aware.

We also apply the same protocol to fundus retinopathy screening (APTOS 2019~\cite{aptos2019}, ViT-DR backbone publicly available on HuggingFace, $n{=}200$ test images, $n{=}400$ val), where the raw backbone exhibits weakly positive $\rho{=}+0.259$ ($p{<}10^{-4}$). Unlike the CheXpert setting, QCTS leaves both $\rho$ and QCDI unchanged ($\text{QCDI}_{\text{QCTS}}{=}+0.130$ vs.\ $\text{QCDI}_{\text{raw}}{=}+0.122$). The null result is expected and informative: the dermoscopy-trained 5-head IQA module is not calibrated to fundus degradation patterns, so the quality scalar that drives QCTS is misaligned with the domain-relevant calibration deficit. The contrast with the CheXpert result illustrates a scope condition for QCTS: it improves quality-aware calibration when the quality scalar captures the degradation dimensions that govern confidence in the target domain, and a domain-specific IQA is required otherwise.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%=====================================================================

The reason existing methods are quality-oblivious is structural. MC Dropout and Deep Ensembles inject uncertainty at the parameter level via stochastic forward passes and independently trained models, which captures epistemic (model) uncertainty but not the input uncertainty that depends on data quality: when a blurry image is presented the parameters are unchanged, so dropout noise and ensemble variance reflect the same distribution as for clean inputs. Input-dependent uncertainty therefore requires an explicit mechanism that conditions the predictive distribution on input properties. QCTS works because it inserts such a mechanism in the simplest possible way; temperature as a function of $\qbar$ adds a quality-dependent confidence penalty without modifying internal representations, separating \emph{what to predict} (logits unchanged) from \emph{how confident to be} (temperature modulates logit scale). The same separation is also QCTS's natural ceiling: logit rescaling cannot repair quality-degraded feature extraction. On HAM10000 zero-shot, Std VIB + QCTS achieves $\rho = -0.108$, an improvement over the uncalibrated $\rho = -0.033$ but still bounded above by what a training-time mechanism could in principle recover. The practical implication is that QCTS is well suited to immediate deployment on already-trained models, with training-time quality conditioning (e.g.\ evidential losses~\cite{sensoy2018evidential}, quality-conditioned prior families) as a complementary direction that we leave to future work.

\textbf{Structural reason TS reverses on weakly quality-aware backbones.}
Standard temperature scaling chooses a single scalar $T$ to minimise validation NLL. Because that scalar is quality-independent, its only degree of freedom along the quality axis is zero, and the optimum is governed by whichever sub-population dominates the validation gradient. On a weakly quality-aware backbone the raw entropy gap between HQ and LQ is small relative to logit magnitude, so the NLL-optimal $T$ contracts predictions toward the mid-confidence regime roughly uniformly. Two regimes follow. If the entropy gap is small --- Std VIB, raw $\rho{=}{-}0.153$ on the validation pool --- uniform contraction drags the LQ--HQ entropy ordering below sampling noise and the ITB-pool $\rho$ flips to $+0.241$. If the gap is wider --- ViT-Tiny, raw $\rho{=}{-}0.160$ but combined with sharper LQ over-confidence --- the entropy-based $\rho$ survives, but the NLL optimum trades HQ ECE for LQ ECE and the sign of QCDI flips ($+0.023 \to -0.029$). On a strongly quality-aware backbone (ResNet-50, raw $\rho{=}{-}0.368$) the entropy--$\qbar$ signal is large enough to absorb a uniform rescale on both axes simultaneously, and TS is neutral. The structural conclusion is that any quality-independent post-hoc correction has exactly zero degrees of freedom along $\qbar$, so it can only inflate or deflate confidence uniformly; quality-aware calibration requires at least one extra degree of freedom along $\qbar$, and QCTS supplies precisely one --- the slope $\alpha$ of $T(\qbar)$ --- which suffices to eliminate both reversal signatures on every backbone we test.

\textbf{Threshold sensitivity and alternative quality proxy.}
The full-pool $\rho$ sign-flip is not a consequence of the specific $\tau{=}0.45$ threshold used to define ITB-LQ. Within the LQ sub-pool ($\qbar < \tau$), standard TS consistently makes $\rho$ \emph{more} negative across all $\tau \in \{0.40, 0.43, 0.45, 0.48, 0.50\}$ (raw $\rho \in [-0.09, -0.01]$ vs.\ TS $\rho \in [-0.19, -0.05]$): TS over-adjusts the HQ tier ($\qbar > 0.50$, $n{=}1671$), where the full-pool reversal is concentrated (HQ raw $\rho{=}{-}0.140 \to$ TS $\rho{=}{+}0.217$), not the LQ tier. To rule out IQA-module specificity, substituting Laplacian variance---a training-free sharpness proxy correlated at $\rho{=}0.50$ with the 5-head IQA scores---produces an even stronger full-pool reversal (raw $\rho{=}{-}0.446 \to$ TS $\rho{=}{+}0.690$) with sign-flips inside every LQ sub-pool, confirming the phenomenon is not tethered to the learned quality estimator.

A manual audit of Std VIB's confidently-wrong predictions on ITB-LQ ($\hat p > 0.85$) is consistent with this picture. The errors split into three modes: heavy blur with indiscernible lesion boundaries (49\%), colour-distorted blur with anomalous colour temperature (32\%), and diagnostically ambiguous lesions (19\%). The first two modes are exactly the dimensions QCTS softens most aggressively in Fig.~\ref{fig:qcts}(b), which suggests that the same prototypes can guide targeted data augmentation in a future training-time fix.

\textbf{Limitations.}
Four caveats bound the scope of our claims. \emph{First, TS reversal is a property of weakly quality-aware backbones, not a universal architectural phenomenon}: on ResNet-50, whose raw representations already encode strong input-quality signal, standard TS is approximately neutral on both $\rho$ and QCDI. The structural argument above predicts which backbones invert, but a complete characterisation across a wider architecture sweep --- ConvNeXt, Swin, large ViTs --- is left to future work. \emph{Second, the LQ partition of ITB is built from programmatic degradations} (Gaussian blur, contrast attacks, brightness and colour shifts) applied to clinically-acquired images, so real-world artefacts such as immersion-gel specular reflections, oblique illumination from a phone torch, or non-frontal framing are not reproduced exactly. As a partial in-the-wild check, we collected 174 real-world low-quality dermoscopy images from publicly available ISIC 2020 low-quality strata; the EfficientNet-B3 backbone achieves ECE$\,{=}\,0.073$ [0.038, 0.125] on these images, lower than ITB-LQ (0.146), suggesting calibration does not collapse on real in-the-wild captures for the benign class. A cross-class evaluation with matched malignant low-quality images remains needed (supplementary). \emph{Third, QCTS is bounded above by the resolution of the external quality model that produces $\qbar$}: training the 5-head IQA module requires a synthetic-degradation labelling stage (Appendix~A, 149{,}100 images) and the module itself can saturate on out-of-distribution artefacts, so the post-hoc temperature can only correct for quality signal that the IQA stage already sees. \emph{Finally, whether a reduced QCDI translates into better triage outcomes is a clinical question} that we do not answer definitively here; however, decision-curve analysis on ITB-LQ shows Std VIB + QCTS achieves a higher maximum net benefit (0.192) than Std VIB alone (0.186), and a triage simulation at a 20\% referral budget reduces the missed-positive rate from 73.3\% to 70.0\% (supplementary). A prospective study remains needed to translate these proxy metrics into clinical impact.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=====================================================================

We have argued that calibration of medical AI should be evaluated as a function of input quality and not only in aggregate, and given the idea three concrete handles: the Quality-Aware Calibration formulation, the QCDI scalar, and a three-class taxonomy of methods. We instantiated these on ITB, a quality-stratified benchmark over four dermoscopy datasets, and proposed QCTS, a two-parameter post-hoc correction that on Std VIB cuts QCDI by 73\% over standard temperature scaling without retraining. The most consequential empirical finding is that the most popular uncertainty methods, MC Dropout and Deep Ensembles, are quality-oblivious despite their reputation: their calibration collapses on exactly the low-quality inputs that medical triage depends on. Natural next steps are to extend ITB beyond dermoscopy with modality-specific quality models, to run a prospective study linking QCDI to triage outcomes, and to derive quality-conditioned variants of richer post-hoc methods such as conformal prediction.
