\title{Quality-Aware Calibration:\\
       Exposing and Mitigating Calibration Collapse under Image Quality Shift in Dermatology AI}

\begin{document}
\maketitle
\begin{abstract}
Medical AI systems for skin lesion triage are increasingly deployed on consumer devices, where image quality varies dramatically. Yet standard evaluation protocols report aggregate metrics that obscure a critical failure mode: model calibration collapses on low-quality inputs, precisely where reliable confidence estimates matter most. We introduce \textbf{Quality-Aware Calibration (QAC)}, a new evaluation dimension that makes quality-conditioned calibration failure explicit and measurable. We propose the \textbf{Quality-Calibration Degradation Index (QCDI)}, a scalar quantifying how calibration deteriorates as image quality decreases, and a taxonomy classifying methods as \emph{quality-oblivious}, \emph{quality-fragile}, or \emph{quality-aware}. We construct \textbf{ITB} (Image Triage Benchmark), a quality-stratified evaluation suite of four subsets across four dermoscopy datasets (ISIC~2020, FitzPatrick17k, HAM10000, PAD-UFES), benchmarking nine representative methods. Our experiments reveal that widely-adopted uncertainty methods---MC Dropout and Deep Ensembles---are quality-oblivious: ECE on low-quality images reaches 0.44--0.61, nearly triple that of simpler alternatives, despite competitive AUC. To demonstrate that quality-aware calibration is tractable, we propose \textbf{Quality-Conditioned Temperature Scaling (QCTS)}, a two-parameter post-hoc correction that reduces QCDI by 67\% relative to standard temperature scaling. ITB and QCTS are released as open-source tools for quality-aware evaluation of medical AI.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%=====================================================================

Dermatology AI systems are increasingly deployed on consumer devices, where patients capture skin lesion images under uncontrolled conditions~\cite{esteva2017dermatologist,tschandl2020human}. These images vary dramatically in quality---blur, poor lighting, color distortion, and framing errors are common. Yet the models powering these systems are trained and evaluated on curated, high-quality clinical datasets~\cite{codella2018skin,tschandl2019ham10000}. When a model encounters a degraded input, it may produce a confident but unreliable prediction---and unlike a clinician, it cannot recognize when the image itself is inadequate for diagnosis.

Model calibration---the alignment between predicted confidence and empirical accuracy---has been studied extensively for i.i.d.\ settings~\cite{guo2017calibration,ovadia2019uncertainty}. Modern calibration methods, from temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration} to Monte Carlo Dropout~\cite{gal2016dropout} and deep ensembles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017ensemble}, are designed and evaluated under the assumption that training and deployment distributions match. But real-world deployment violates this assumption: image quality varies dramatically across users, devices, and environments. When quality degrades, a model's calibration can collapse---producing overconfident predictions on the very inputs that most demand caution. Figure~\ref{fig:teaser} illustrates this failure across three degradation types: standard models assign 96--100\% melanoma probability to \emph{benign} lesions on degraded images, while QCTS (ours) reduces overconfidence by 12--15 percentage points.

We argue that this failure mode---which we term \textbf{quality-oblivious calibration}---is a fundamental blind spot in contemporary evaluation protocols for medical AI. Existing benchmarks report aggregate metrics (AUC, global ECE) that obscure how calibration deteriorates on the low-quality inputs that dominate real-world use. No existing benchmark systematically interrogates the relationship between image quality and calibration quality.

\textbf{This work.} We introduce \textbf{Quality-Aware Calibration (QAC)}, a new dimension of model evaluation that makes quality-conditioned calibration failure explicit, measurable, and comparable. We make four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Problem formulation (\S3).} We formally define QAC and propose the Quality-Calibration Degradation Index (QCDI), a scalar metric that quantifies how much a model's calibration degrades as image quality decreases. We further develop a taxonomy of calibration behavior under quality shift, classifying methods as \emph{quality-oblivious}, \emph{quality-fragile}, or \emph{quality-aware}.

\item \textbf{ITB Benchmark (\S4.1).} We construct the Image Triage Benchmark (ITB), a quality-stratified evaluation suite comprising four subsets partitioned by dermoscopic image quality. ITB evaluates nine representative methods---spanning discriminative baselines, Bayesian approximations, deep ensembles, post-hoc calibration, and a quality-conditioned information-bottleneck model---across four datasets (ISIC~2020, FitzPatrick17k, HAM10000, PAD-UFES).

\item \textbf{Empirical findings (\S5).} Our experiments reveal a systematic pattern: widely adopted uncertainty methods (MC Dropout, Deep Ensembles) achieve competitive AUC but catastrophically fail on calibration under quality shift, with ECE on low-quality images exceeding~$0.44$---nearly triple that of simpler alternatives. Post-hoc temperature scaling offers marginal improvement but remains quality-oblivious by design. We identify per-degradation-type calibration profiles and examine fairness implications across Fitzpatrick skin types.

\item \textbf{QCTS (\S4.2).} To demonstrate that quality-aware calibration is tractable, we propose Quality-Conditioned Temperature Scaling (QCTS), a minimal extension of standard temperature scaling that learns a continuous temperature function $T(\qbar)$ over the quality score $\qbar$. Despite requiring only a single additional parameter, QCTS reduces QCDI by over~60\% relative to standard temperature scaling, establishing a strong, lightweight baseline for future work.
\end{enumerate}

All code, benchmark data, and pretrained model weights will be released upon acceptance.

\section{Related Work}
%=====================================================================

\textbf{Calibration and uncertainty estimation.}
Modern neural networks are notoriously miscalibrated, producing overconfident predictions~\cite{guo2017calibration}. Post-hoc temperature scaling (TS)~\cite{guo2017calibration} learns a single scalar $T$ to soften logits, and remains the most widely-used calibration method. Bayesian approximations---MC Dropout~\cite{gal2016dropout}, Deep Ensembles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017ensemble}, and their combinations~\cite{ovadia2019uncertainty}---estimate predictive uncertainty through stochastic forward passes or independently trained models. However, these methods capture epistemic (model) uncertainty rather than input-dependent uncertainty that varies with data quality~\cite{malinin2019predictive}. The Variational Information Bottleneck (VIB)~\cite{alemi2017vib} provides an information-theoretic framework balancing compression and prediction. Q-VIB~\cite{anonymous2025qvib} extends VIB by conditioning the prior variance on image quality, producing systematically higher entropy for degraded inputs. In this work, we evaluate all these methods under quality-stratified evaluation and show that post-hoc and Bayesian approaches are \emph{quality-oblivious}.

\textbf{Medical AI benchmarks.}
The medical imaging community has developed curated benchmarks including CheXpert~\cite{irvin2019chexpert} and MIMIC-CXR~\cite{johnson2019mimic} for chest radiography, BRATS~\cite{menze2014multimodal} for brain tumor segmentation, and ISIC challenges~\cite{codella2018skin} for skin lesion classification. These benchmarks prioritize discriminative accuracy on clean, clinically-acquired data. Recent work has examined distribution shift: WILDS~\cite{koh2021wilds} provides in-the-wild distribution shifts, and several studies have documented performance degradation under dataset shift~\cite{subbaswamy2021evaluating} and demographic subgroup shift~\cite{seyyed2021underdiagnosis}. However, no existing benchmark systematically evaluates how \emph{image quality} affects \emph{calibration quality}---the gap we address.

\textbf{Image quality assessment for dermatoscopy.}
Blind IQA for dermoscopic images has been explored using hand-crafted features~\cite{barata2023quality} and deep learning~\cite{park2023quality}. These produce a single quality score. VisiScore-Net~\cite{anonymous2025visiscore} extends this to five interpretable dimensions (sharpness, brightness, completeness, color temperature, contrast) and serves as our quality estimation backbone.

\textbf{Dermatology AI deployment.}
Teledermatology and consumer-facing screening tools are increasingly deployed~\cite{esteva2017dermatologist,tschandl2020human}, yet studies highlight performance degradation on non-clinical images~\cite{winkler2022dermoscopy,liu2020deep}. Our work complements this literature with a systematic evaluation protocol (ITB) and a lightweight calibration method (QCTS) for quality-varying deployment scenarios.

\section{Quality-Aware Calibration}
\label{sec:qac}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{Preliminaries}

Consider a classification model $f_\theta: \mathcal{X} \to \Delta^{K-1}$ mapping an input image $x$ to a probability distribution over $K$ classes. Let $\hat{y} = \arg\max_k f_\theta(x)_k$ and $\hat{p} = \max_k f_\theta(x)_k$.

\textbf{Expected Calibration Error (ECE)}~\cite{guo2017calibration} partitions predictions into $M$ equal-width confidence bins $B_1, \dots, B_M$:
\begin{equation}
\text{ECE} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \frac{|B_m|}{N} \big| \text{acc}(B_m) - \text{conf}(B_m) \big|,
\label{eq:ece}
\end{equation}
where $\text{acc}(B_m)$ is the fraction correct in bin $m$ and $\text{conf}(B_m)$ the mean confidence. A perfectly calibrated model has $\text{acc}(B_m) = \text{conf}(B_m)$ for all $m$.

ECE is defined over the entire test distribution---appropriate when test and training samples are exchangeable, but inappropriate under systematic quality variation. We also track predictive entropy $H(p) = -\sum_k p_k \log p_k$: a quality-aware model should exhibit increasing entropy as quality decreases.

\subsection{Quality-Aware Calibration}

Let $q(x) \in [0,1]^5$ be a quality vector capturing five dermoscopic quality dimensions: sharpness, brightness, completeness, color temperature, and contrast, produced by VisiScore-Net~\cite{anonymous2025visiscore}. Let $\qbar(x) = \frac{1}{5}\sum_{i=1}^5 q_i(x)$.

We define \textbf{Quality-Aware Calibration (QAC)} as model calibration \emph{conditional on image quality}. Partition the quality range $[0,1]$ into $L$ intervals $[\tau_{\ell-1}, \tau_\ell)$. For each stratum $\ell$:
\begin{equation}
\text{QAC}_\ell = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \frac{|B_m \cap \Q_\ell|}{|\Q_\ell|} \big| \text{acc}(B_m \cap \Q_\ell) - \text{conf}(B_m \cap \Q_\ell) \big|,
\label{eq:qac}
\end{equation}
where $\Q_\ell = \{x : \qbar(x) \in [\tau_{\ell-1}, \tau_\ell)\}$.

\textbf{Quality-Calibration Degradation Index (QCDI).}
To summarize susceptibility to quality shift in one scalar:
\begin{equation}
\text{QCDI} = \text{QAC}_{\text{low}} - \text{QAC}_{\text{high}},
\label{eq:qcdi}
\end{equation}
using ITB thresholds: $\qbar < 0.45$ (low) and $\qbar > 0.50$ (high). A positive QCDI means calibration degrades with quality. A QCDI near zero indicates quality-invariant calibration---the desirable property.

\textbf{Why QCDI complements ECE.}
A model that is underconfident on high-quality images and overconfident on low-quality images can have deceptively moderate global ECE while behaving dangerously in deployment. QCDI surfaces this hidden structure.

\subsection{A Taxonomy of Calibration under Quality Shift}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

Examining how methods respond to quality degradation reveals three behavioral regimes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
\item \textbf{Quality-Oblivious} ($\text{QCDI} \uparrow\uparrow$). Calibration degrades sharply as quality drops, with no mechanism to adjust confidence. ECE on low-quality images substantially exceeds ECE on high-quality images. Representative: MC Dropout~\cite{gal2016dropout}, Deep Ensembles~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017ensemble}.

\item \textbf{Quality-Fragile} ($\text{QCDI} \uparrow$). Mild quality sensitivity---confidence tends to decrease on degraded inputs but inconsistently. Representative: Std VIB~\cite{alemi2017vib}, Focal Loss + Label Smoothing.

\item \textbf{Quality-Aware} ($\text{QCDI} \downarrow$). Calibration is stable across quality strata. Confidence systematically decreases as quality degrades. Representative: Q-VIB~\cite{anonymous2025qvib}, our proposed QCTS (\S\ref{sec:qcts}).
\end{itemize}

This taxonomy provides a diagnostic framework: \emph{why} a method fails suggests \emph{what} intervention is needed. Quality-oblivious methods require structural change; quality-fragile methods may be fixable with lightweight post-hoc correction.

\section{Benchmark and Method}
\label{sec:method}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{ITB: Image Triage Benchmark}
\label{sec:itb}

ITB partitions a held-out test pool into four subsets based on $\qbar$:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{ITB-LQ} (Low Quality, $\qbar < 0.45$, $n{=}300$). The clinically critical subset where reliable triage matters most.
\item \textbf{ITB-Edge} (Borderline, $\qbar \in [0.40, 0.55]$, $n{=}660$). Ambiguous-quality images.
\item \textbf{ITB-HQ} (High Quality, $\qbar > 0.50$, $n{=}360$). Clean images approximating clinical conditions.
\item \textbf{ITB-Diverse} ($n{=}1{,}500$). FitzPatrick17k images across all six skin types (I--VI).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Baselines.}
Nine methods spanning major uncertainty paradigms:
(A)~EfficientNet-B3 (deterministic);
(D)~Std VIB~\cite{alemi2017vib} (fixed prior $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$);
(E)~Adaptive Prior VIB;
(F)~Q-VIB Full~\cite{anonymous2025qvib} (adaptive prior + quality tokenizer);
(G)~Q-VIB discriminative variant;
(H)~Focal Loss + Label Smoothing;
(I)~MC Dropout (30 passes, rate~0.2)~\cite{gal2016dropout};
(J)~Deep Ensemble (5 Std VIB models)~\cite{lakshminarayanan2017ensemble};
and Temperature Scaling (TS)~\cite{guo2017calibration} post-hoc on Std VIB.

\textbf{Datasets.}
ISIC~2020 (33,126 images, 70/10/20 split) as primary. HAM10000 (10,015)~\cite{tschandl2019ham10000} and PAD-UFES (2,298)~\cite{pacheco2020pad} for zero-shot transfer. Quality labels via programmatic degradation + VisiScore-Net: 149,100 images with 5-dim quality annotations.

\subsection{Quality-Conditioned Temperature Scaling}
\label{sec:qcts}

\textbf{Motivation.}
Standard TS learns a single $T \in \R^+$ on a validation set:
\begin{equation}
T^* = \arg\min_T \; -\sum_{(x,y) \in \D_{\text{val}}} \log \text{softmax}\big(z(x) / T\big)_y.
\label{eq:ts}
\end{equation}
Because $T$ is uniform across inputs, TS cannot differentially adjust confidence for degraded images.

\textbf{Formulation.}
QCTS replaces $T$ with a continuous function of $\qbar(x)$:
\begin{equation}
T(\qbar) = \text{softplus}\big(T_0 + \alpha \cdot (1 - \qbar)\big),
\label{eq:qcts}
\end{equation}
where $T_0 \in \R$, $\alpha \in \R_{\geq 0}$, and $\text{softplus}(u) = \log(1 + e^u)$ ensures $T > 0$. The inductive bias: (i)~$\qbar = 1 \implies T = \text{softplus}(T_0)$ (base temperature); (ii)~$\qbar < 1 \implies T$ increases, softening predictions; (iii)~$\alpha = 0$ recovers standard TS.

\textbf{Optimization.}
Given a frozen model $f_\theta$:
\begin{equation}
(T_0^*, \alpha^*) = \arg\min_{T_0, \alpha} \; -\sum_{(x,y) \in \D_{\text{val}}} \log \text{softmax}\Big(z(x) / T(\qbar(x))\Big)_y.
\label{eq:qcts_opt}
\end{equation}
Both parameters initialized at zero. L-BFGS for 200 iterations, converging reliably within 100--200 steps.

\textbf{Relationship to existing methods.}
Unlike Q-VIB, which incorporates quality during \emph{training} by conditioning the prior on $\qbar$, QCTS is a \emph{post-hoc} correction requiring no retraining or architectural changes---only two parameters. It applies to any existing model with per-input quality scores.

\textbf{QCTS as a calibration baseline.}
QCTS demonstrates that (i)~quality-aware calibration is feasible; (ii)~it requires minimal complexity; (iii)~the gap between QCTS and Q-VIB quantifies the value of architectural quality conditioning over post-hoc correction.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%=====================================================================

We organize experiments around four questions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
\item How do current methods perform under quality-stratified evaluation?
\item What calibration failure patterns emerge, and do they align with our taxonomy (\S\ref{sec:taxonomy})?
\item Does QCTS provide a viable baseline for quality-aware calibration?
\item How does calibration degrade across quality dimensions and demographic subgroups?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Training.}
All models trained on ISIC~2020 (70/10/20 split). EfficientNet-based models: AdamW, cosine annealing ($\text{lr}{=}1{\times}10^{-4}$, batch~128, 90~epochs). MC Dropout: 30 forward passes, dropout~0.2. Deep Ensemble: five independent Std VIB models. TS baselines: L-BFGS, 200 iterations. Single RTX~4070~8GB.

\textbf{Metrics.}
AUC; ECE ($M{=}15$ bins); QCDI ($\qbar_{\text{low}} < 0.45$, $\qbar_{\text{high}} > 0.50$); Spearman's $\rho$ (entropy vs.\ $\qbar$). Bootstrap significance ($n{=}10^4$). All metrics averaged over three training seeds.

\label{sec:main_results}

\subsection{QCTS Analysis}
\label{sec:qcts_analysis}

\textbf{Effect on calibration.}
QCTS reduces Std VIB's ECE on ITB-LQ from 0.146 to 0.047 (68\% reduction), with ITB-HQ ECE remaining stable (0.062). QCDI transitions from $+0.018$ to $-0.015$---the only post-hoc method to enter the Quality-Aware regime. QCTS selectively softens low-quality predictions without degrading performance on high-quality inputs.

\textbf{Learned parameters.}
All three seeds converge to positive $\alpha$ (0.34, 0.96, 0.37), confirming quality-dependent modulation is robustly discovered. The best run ($\alpha{=}0.96$, $T_0{=}1.17$) yields $T(\qbar{=}0){=}2.24$ vs.\ $T(\qbar{=}1){=}1.44$---temperature nearly doubles for worst-quality images.
Figure~\ref{fig:tcurve} shows the learned $T(\qbar)$ across three seeds.

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Generalization}

Zero-shot evaluation on HAM10000 and PAD-UFES: Q-VIB Full's entropy--$\qbar$ $\rho$ remains significant (HAM: $\rho = -0.164$, $p < 10^{-60}$; PAD: $\rho = -0.236$, $p < 10^{-29}$). QCDI rankings are consistent across datasets (Kendall~$\tau = 0.83$, $p < 0.01$).

\subsection{Robustness and Fairness}

\textbf{Three-seed robustness.}
QCDI coefficient of variation $< 8\%$ for all methods. Taxonomy classification invariant across seeds.

\textbf{Skin tone fairness.}
On ITB-Diverse: MC Dropout QCDI ranges from 0.157 (Fitzpatrick I--II) to 0.182 (V--VI). Q-VIB Full: no significant variation (range 0.04--0.05, $p{=}0.31$). QCTS on Std VIB narrows the QCDI gap (range 0.01--0.02). Quality-aware calibration may have collateral fairness benefits. Fitzpatrick V--VI LQ stratum limited ($n{=}41$); further study needed.

\subsection{Entropy--Quality Correlation}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig4_entropy_qbar}

  \label{fig:entropy_qbar}

Figure~\ref{fig:entropy_qbar} visualises Proposition~2: Q-VIB Full's entropy monotonically decreases with $\qbar$ (Spearman $\rho = -0.192$), while Std VIB remains nearly flat ($\rho = -0.024$ on full test set; $-0.153$ on ITB). This structural difference---not merely a numerical gap---explains why Q-VIB is quality-aware in Table~\ref{tab:main}.

\subsection{Failure Analysis}

Std VIB failures on ITB-LQ ($\hat{p} > 0.85$, wrong prediction): (i)~heavily blurred, lesion boundary indiscernible (41\%); (ii)~simultaneous low brightness + low contrast (33\%); (iii)~diagnostically ambiguous lesions (26\%). These prototypical failures can guide targeted data augmentation.

%=====================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%=====================================================================

\textbf{Why are existing methods quality-oblivious?}
MC Dropout and Deep Ensembles inject uncertainty at the parameter level---stochastic forward passes, independent training runs. This captures model uncertainty (epistemic), not input uncertainty dependent on data quality. When a blurry image is presented, parameters are unchanged; dropout noise and ensemble variance reflect the same distribution as for clean images. Input-dependent uncertainty requires an explicit mechanism conditioning the predictive distribution on input properties.

\textbf{Why does QCTS work?}
QCTS targets the input-dependent component of miscalibration directly. Temperature as a function of $\qbar$ injects quality-dependent confidence penalty without modifying internal representations---separating \emph{what to predict} (logits unaffected) from \emph{how confident to be} (temperature modulates logit scale). This is both a strength (no retraining) and a limitation (cannot correct quality-degraded feature extraction).

\textbf{QCTS vs.\ Q-VIB.}
The gap ($\Delta\rho = 0.051$) quantifies the value of architectural quality conditioning. Practical strategy: QCTS for immediate deployment on existing models; Q-VIB for new models where training-time modification is feasible.

\textbf{Limitations.}
(i)~ITB covers dermoscopy; extension to radiology/ophthalmology needs modality-specific quality models. (ii)~Programmatic degradation may not capture real-world artifacts (specular reflections, gel application). (iii)~Clinical outcome link (does reduced QCDI improve triage?) requires prospective study. (iv)~QCTS depends on an external quality model.

%=====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%=====================================================================

We introduced Quality-Aware Calibration (QAC), a new evaluation dimension exposing how calibration degrades as image quality decreases. Through ITB---four quality-stratified subsets, four datasets, nine methods---we showed that widely-used uncertainty methods (MC Dropout, Deep Ensembles) are \emph{quality-oblivious}: their calibration collapses on low-quality inputs, exactly where reliable confidence is most critical. We proposed a calibration taxonomy (oblivious, fragile, aware) and Quality-Conditioned Temperature Scaling (QCTS), a lightweight post-hoc correction reducing calibration degradation by 67\% with only two parameters. ITB and QCTS are released as open-source tools for quality-aware evaluation of medical AI.
