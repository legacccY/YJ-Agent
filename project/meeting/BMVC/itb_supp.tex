\section{5-Head IQA Module}
\label{app:iqa}
%=====================================================================

The per-image quality scalar $\qbar(x) \in [0,1]$ used throughout this paper is the mean of five independently trained quality heads, each predicting one dermoscopic quality dimension.

\textbf{Quality dimensions.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item $q_1$: \emph{Sharpness}. Blurring reduces lesion boundary definition, the most calibration-critical degradation (see Fig.~3b in the main paper).
  \item $q_2$: \emph{Brightness}. Under- or over-exposure affects the lesion-skin contrast perceived by the model.
  \item $q_3$: \emph{Completeness}. Partial coverage — lesion cropped at image boundary — degrades diagnosis reliability.
  \item $q_4$: \emph{Colour temperature}. White-balance errors shift hue distributions, though the calibration effect is comparatively mild.
  \item $q_5$: \emph{Contrast}. Flattened local contrast reduces texture signal, particularly for pigment network features.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Training procedure.}
Each head is a linear regressor on top of a frozen EfficientNet-B0 backbone pre-trained on ImageNet. We generate 149,100 (image, quality) pairs by applying programmatic degradations to the ISIC~2020 training set: for each dimension, we uniformly sample a degradation severity $s \in [0,1]$ and apply the corresponding transformation (Gaussian blur, brightness scaling, random crop, colour temperature shift, contrast scaling), then set $q_i = 1 - s$ as the ground-truth label. Each head is trained independently with mean-squared-error loss, 10 epochs, AdamW ($\text{lr}{=}3{\times}10^{-4}$), batch 256.

\textbf{Evaluation.}
On a held-out validation set of 29,820 image-quality pairs:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{5-head IQA module per-dimension performance.}
\label{tab:iqa_perf}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Dimension & PLCC & SRCC & RMSE \\
\midrule
Sharpness ($q_1$)        & 0.947 & 0.863 & 0.089 \\
Brightness ($q_2$)       & 0.987 & 0.986 & 0.041 \\
Completeness ($q_3$)     & 0.731 & 0.689 & 0.171 \\
Colour temp.\ ($q_4$)    & 0.992 & 0.990 & 0.028 \\
Contrast ($q_5$)         & 0.961 & 0.945 & 0.063 \\

\bottomrule

The module achieves high PLCC/SRCC on four of five dimensions; completeness is harder because partial framing has weaker texture gradients. The mean quality scalar $\qbar = \frac{1}{5}\sum_i q_i$ is used as the QCTS input; this averaging suppresses noise in any single dimension.

\section{Decision-Curve Analysis and Triage Simulation}
\label{app:dca}
%=====================================================================

The decision-curve analysis (DCA) and triage simulation referenced in Section~6 (Limitations) of the main paper are detailed here.

\textbf{Decision-Curve Analysis (DCA).}
DCA evaluates a model's clinical utility by computing the net benefit (NB) as a function of a decision threshold $t$:
\[
\text{NB}(t) = \frac{\text{TP}(t)}{n} - \frac{\text{FP}(t)}{n} \cdot \frac{t}{1 - t}
\]
where $n$ is the total number of patients, TP and FP are true and false positives at threshold $t$. A higher NB at a clinically relevant $t$ indicates better utility.
