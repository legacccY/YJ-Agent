\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

We organise prior work into four threads that our method intersects:
tubular/vessel segmentation backbones, topology- and connectivity-oriented
objectives, linear-attention and delta-rule sequence models, and
differentiable vesselness priors. Throughout, we emphasise that none of these
threads performs \emph{in-model spatial re-identification of the same vessel
across a break}, which is the locus of our contribution.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Vessel and Tubular Structure Segmentation}
\label{sec:related:vessel}

\paragraph{CNN backbones.}
Curvilinear and tubular structure segmentation has been driven by encoder--decoder
CNNs. Beyond U-Net and its variants (UNet++, Attention U-Net), specialised designs
target the thin, elongated geometry of vessels: IterNet~\cite{iternet}, SA-UNet,
CS$^2$-Net~\cite{csnet}, SCS-Net, and FR-UNet~\cite{frunet} refine multi-scale
context, attention, or iterative refinement. These models improve the per-pixel
delineation of vessels but treat each forward pass as a single bottom-up labelling
problem; they carry no explicit notion of vessel \emph{identity} that could be
retrieved across a low-contrast or occluded gap.

\paragraph{State-space (Mamba) backbones.}
A recent line replaces or augments convolutional context with selective
state-space models (SSMs). Generic medical backbones include VM-UNet~\cite{vmunet},
U-Mamba~\cite{umamba}, and Swin-UMamba~\cite{swinumamba}. Vessel- and
tubular-specific SSM designs are more directly comparable: HM-Mamba couples
hierarchical multi-scale tubular convolution with frequency-domain gating;
VFGS-Net~\cite{vfgsnet} introduces a frequency-guided bidirectional scan;
MM-UNet~\cite{mmunet} adds morphological deformable sampling;
MambaVesselNet++~\cite{mambavesselpp} targets 3D vessels with bifocal fusion;
and TA-Mamba combines serpentine spatial convolution with frequency-aware gated
Mamba while reporting topology-aware scores. A separate group emphasises the
\emph{scan ordering} itself, e.g. the serpentine scan of Serp-Mamba~\cite{serpmamba}
on wide-field fundus and the diagonal scan of TopoMamba~\cite{topomamba}. These
methods establish strong segmentation baselines and we compare against them
directly. Crucially, the recurrent state in these SSM backbones aggregates
\emph{context} along a fixed scan; it is not used as an addressable
key--value memory that stores and retrieves a specific vessel's identity.
We adopt a standard reorder for 2D scanning and explicitly do \emph{not}
claim the scan as a contribution (R4); our contribution is the associative
memory operating on the scanned sequence, not the scan itself.

\subsection{Topology- and Connectivity-Oriented Methods}
\label{sec:related:topo}

A complementary body of work enforces connectivity through dedicated objectives
or architectural priors rather than through per-pixel overlap. clDice~\cite{cldice}
and its radius-aware extension cbDice~\cite{cbdice} provide differentiable
soft-skeleton losses; persistent-homology objectives such as
TopoLoss~\cite{topoloss}, Betti Matching~\cite{bettimatching}, and the warping
loss~\cite{warpingloss} penalise topological discrepancies; Skeleton
Recall~\cite{skeletonrecall} offers a lightweight CPU skeleton objective; and
Topograph~\cite{topograph} encodes a component graph. On the architectural side,
DSCNet~\cite{dscnet} adapts the receptive field to curved tubular shapes,
DconnNet~\cite{dconnnet} decouples directional connectivity subspaces, and
PASC-Net~\cite{pascnet} couples plug-and-play shape convolutions with hierarchical
topological constraints.

These approaches share two properties that distinguish them from ours. First, the
loss-based methods (clDice, cbDice, Betti/persistent-homology, Skeleton Recall) act
as global differentiable \emph{regularisers}: they push the prediction toward the
correct global topology but do not localise a break or carry an in-model record of
which structure should be continued. Second, the architectural priors model
directional or shape context within a single feed-forward pass and likewise hold no
cross-gap identity state. Most directly related, the learned post-processing model
of Corvo~\emph{et al.}~\cite{creatis} restores connectivity by training a residual
U-Net that operates on the \emph{segmentation mask} produced by an upstream
segmenter; it is an explicit two-stage, post-hoc reconstruction without end-to-end
gradients into the segmentation backbone and without an associative memory. We adopt
its synthetic-break generation protocol for our reconnection benchmark
(Section~\ref{sec:bench}), but our reconnection mechanism is fundamentally different:
it is performed \emph{inside} the forward pass of a single end-to-end model via a
delta-rule matrix state, rather than as a downstream correction of a mask.

\subsection{Linear Attention and Delta-Rule Sequence Models}
\label{sec:related:delta}

Linear-attention and delta-rule models replace softmax attention with a recurrent
matrix-valued state that is updated in linear time. DeltaNet~\cite{deltanet}
introduces a delta update rule with a parallel Householder formulation; gated linear
attention (GLA)~\cite{gla} and Mamba-2~\cite{mamba2} add data-dependent gating and
unify SSMs with attention; RWKV~\cite{rwkv} and test-time training~\cite{ttt}
explore matrix-valued or gradient-updated hidden states. Gated DeltaNet
(GDN-1)~\cite{gatedeltanet} unifies the delta update with a scalar decay gate.
Our memory module is built on its successor, Gated DeltaNet-2
(GDN-2)~\cite{gdn2}, which decouples the gating into a channel-wise erase gate
on the key side and a write gate on the value side, with GDN-1 recovered as a
degenerate special case. To the best of our knowledge, delta-rule associative
memory of this kind has so far been evaluated only on language modelling and
long-context retrieval; it has not previously been applied to visual or
segmentation tasks.

\paragraph{Distinction from GDKVM.}
The closest neighbour to our use of gated delta memory is GDKVM~\cite{gdkvm}.
Unlike GDKVM [ICCV25] which uses gated delta KV memory across echo video frames
(temporal), we operate within a single image (spatial) and add explicit
same-vessel re-identification. Concretely, GDKVM stores a key--value memory and
builds \emph{inter-frame} associations to segment cardiac chambers in
echocardiography \emph{video}, where the memory is indexed by time; our setting
has no temporal axis. We index the memory along a spatial scan of a single fundus
image and read it out to decide whether the two sides of a break belong to the
\emph{same} vessel. We are not aware of any prior method that uses delta-rule
associative memory to perform in-model reconnection of vessel breaks, which
positions our work as a distinct point in this space.

\subsection{Differentiable Vesselness Priors}
\label{sec:related:frangi}

Hessian-based vesselness filters (e.g. Frangi's measure) are classical
input-derived cues for tubular structures. Frangi-Net~\cite{frangiNet}
reformulates the 2D Frangi filter as a trainable network so that the Frangi
\emph{weights themselves are learned to perform segmentation} -- there the Frangi
computation is the backbone. Other works use vesselness as a preprocessing or
pseudo-labelling signal: as a self-supervised pretext~\cite{frangiPretext} or as a
filtered input channel fused with the image~\cite{frangiFusion}. Our use is
orthogonal to all of these: we keep a differentiable Frangi layer as a side-branch
that consumes only the input image, and we use its \emph{output as an external gate
signal that modulates the erase/write strength} of the GDN-2 associative memory
(Section~\ref{sec:method:frangi}). The vesselness is never used as a label, a
segmentation prediction, or a backbone; it is a derived, input-only control signal
for the memory. We are not aware of prior work that couples a Hessian vesselness
measure into the gating of a linear-attention memory in this way.
