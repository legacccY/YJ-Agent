038 1 Introduction                                                                                                                038

039 Problem. Diagnostic models for skin lesions are typically trained on curated 039

040 dermoscopic images, yet real consumer-device photographs span a wide quality 040

041 range -- motion blur, poor illumination, incomplete framing, colour casts, and 041

042 low contrast -- on which a model accurate on clean data can be both wrong and 042

043 confident. The deployment question is therefore not only "what is the diagnosis?" 043

044 but "is this image good enough to diagnose, and if not, what should the system 044

045 do about it?"                                                                                                                 045

046 Why existing tools fall short. Three families each address part of the prob- 046
047 lem but none closes the loop: (i) post-hoc calibration (temperature scaling [12], 047
048 Platt/isotonic) and (ii) uncertainty estimation (MC dropout [8], deep ensembles 048
049 [18]) both yield quality-oblivious confidence, unable to attribute it to degrada- 049
050 tion rather than genuine ambiguity; (iii) generic enhancement (Real-ESRGAN 050
051 [28], Restormer [30]) optimises perception with no guarantee that diagnostic 051
052 content survives, and generative restoration risks inventing clinically unaccept- 052
053 able structure (pigment networks, vessels). None preserves the diagnostic signal 053
                                           ACCV 2026 Submission #*****      3

054 under a guarantee; gradient-level repair [32] treats the symptom during train- 054

055 ing, whereas we bound the information loss directly and decide, per input, when 055

056 enhancement should be withheld in favour of re-acquisition.                056

057 Closed-loop quality-triage. We argue that quality must be a first-class signal 057

058 threaded through the whole pipeline. Our system forms a closed loop: 5-head 058

059 IQAassesses quality; the agent decides whether to diagnose directly, diagnose 059

060 with caution, enhance-then-diagnose with our diagnosis-preserving enhancer QP- 060

061 Enhance, or query for a retake; and a frozen classifier produces the final diagnosis, 061

062 its posterior confidence being the signal the gate routes on (a standard bottleneck 062

063 posterior suffices, º7.8). Crucially, the driving quality scalar need not come from 063

064 5-head IQAalone: any per-input estimate -- a learned scorer, a no-reference IQA 064

065 metric, or a known corruption severity -- keeps the framework general.     065

097 2 Related Work                                                               097

098 Enhancement and the perception¡diagnosis gap. Strong deterministic 098

099 restoration backbones (Real-ESRGAN [28], Restormer [30], NAFNet [5], MIRNet- 099

100 v2 [31], SwinIR [20], Uformer [29]) optimise distortion or perceptual fidelity, but 100

101 high fidelity need not preserve diagnosis. Generative super-resolution can even 101

102 synthesise pigment-network or vascular structure that was never present, a der- 102

103 moscopy safety hazard; we therefore use deterministic restoration and analyse 103

104 this failure mode in º8. GradProm [32] documents the same gap in the skin-lesion 104

105 (ISIC) domain and repairs gradients during training. We share its diagnosis but 105

106 differ in guarantee and scope:                                               106

107     Unlike GradProm, which corrects gradients during training, we provide    107

108     (i) a mutual-information lower bound guarantee for diagnosis preserva-   108

109     tion (Lemma 1), and (ii) a routing decision including query-for-retake.  109

110     GradProm treats the symptom by repairing gradients; we bound the         110

111     information loss and decide when enhancement is unsafe.                  111

112 We cite GradProm as prior evidence of the gap and differentiate on mechanism, 112

113 running no head-to-head comparison and claiming no superiority. Across these 113

114 families, none preserves the diagnostic signal under a guarantee: we bound the 114

115 information loss (Lemma 1) and route on it.                                  115

116     Perception¡distortion trade-offs. Blau and Michaeli [4] bound the perception¡116

117 distortion trade-off, and Cohen et al. [6] show that distribution-matching trans- 117

118 lation can add or remove pathology-bearing features. We instead make diagnostic 118

119 fidelity the explicitly preserved quantity, with a mutual-information lower bound 119

120 (Lemma 1) characterising the moderate-degradation window in which enhance- 120

121 ment costs no diagnostic information.                                        121

122     Selective prediction, deferral, and the recapture gap. Selective clas- 122

123 sification abstains below a confidence threshold [10, 11], and learning-to-defer 123

124 hands the case to a clinician; both treat the input as fixed. Medical image- 124

125 quality work instead flags poor capture and prompts re-photography (teleder- 125

126 matology [15, 27], point-of-care ultrasound [3]), supplying clinical motivation 126

127 but leaving the retake decision decoupled from any diagnostic-risk estimate. 127

128 Our query-for-retake channel closes this gap: the agent (º5) routes on a quality 128

129 estimate and, when the image is too degraded for enhancement to be safe, re- 129

130 quests re-acquisition -- a re-acquisition loop that is, to our knowledge, the first 130

131 to be bounded by a local diagnostic-risk guarantee and coupled jointly with the 131

132 diagnostic decision, rather than triggered by a quality flag in isolation. Adjacent 132

133 lines that might appear to subsume it -- active perception and next-best-view 133

134 planning, active sensing and active feature acquisition, and human-in-the-loop 134

135 recapture -- each select a sensing or measurement action, but none couples that 135

136 decision to a per-input diagnostic-risk estimate with a local risk bound, and 136

137 so none falls within the risk-bounded, diagnosis-coupled routing framework we 137

138 study here. We make no claim that this routing raises net benefit globally; º7.8 138

139 reports where it does and does not help.                                     139

192 4 Diagnosis-Preserving Enhancement                           192

193 Of the five quality dimensions, four (sharpness, brightness, colour temperature, 193

194 and contrast) admit deterministic restoration, whereas completeness (q3, a trun- 194

195 cated field of view) is only partially recoverable: enhancement cannot synthe- 195

196 sise tissue that was never captured, so the agent treats low completeness as a 196

197 query-for-retake signal rather than an enhancement target. This split -- which 197

198 dimensions are recoverable, and within what severity window -- is the boundary 198

199 this section formalises and Sec. 7 measures.                 199

200 4.1 Architecture: NAFNet with quality-conditional FiLM       200

201 QP-Enhance is a NAFNet backbone [5] with FiLM modulation [25] conditioned 201

202 on the per-input quality vector, so restoration strength adapts to how an image 202

203 is degraded rather than applying a fixed correction. The restoration map T(x, q) 203

204 is deterministic: we avoid generative restoration (diffusion or adversarial back- 204

205 bones), because such models can hallucinate diagnostic structure -- inventing 205

206 a pigment network or vascular pattern that was never present -- which is un- 206

207 acceptable clinically (Sec. 8 analyses why diffusion fails here). FiLM is not a 207

208 fidelity mechanism: its empirical role (E8, Sec. 7) is quality-conditional diagnos- 208

209 tic fidelity rather than perceptual sharpening.              209

259 5 Query-for-Retake Agent                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              259

260 The restorer of Sec. 4 is only safe to apply within the salvage band. Outside 260

261 it, enhancement either does nothing useful or, as our boundary evidence shows 261

262 (Sec. 7.7), harms the diagnosis. The agent therefore does not diagnose: from a 262

263 per-input quality reading it decides whether to diagnose directly, to enhance and 263

264 then diagnose, or to ask for a better image. The last channel is the mechanistic 264

265 contribution of this section. Existing reliability mechanisms (selective prediction, 265

266 learning-to-defer, and the agentic pipeline AT-CXR; Sec. 2) stop the moment an 266

267 input is judged unsafe, abstaining or deferring without acting on the cause of 267

268 the failure -- a recoverably bad acquisition. The query-for-retake channel instead 268

269 closes a collection loop back to the sensor, requesting a better image rather than 269

270 abstaining on or deferring the present one.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           270

439 7.7 The Honest Boundary: When Enhancement Hurts Diagnosis                                       439

440 A per-class split (Fig. 2A) is where the salvage story collapses. The benign and 440

441 malignant subgroups behave in opposite directions: enhancement salvages most 441

442 degradation-misclassified benign lesions (75.6%, 1809/2392) while damaging al- 442

443 most none, yet on melanoma it salvages only 5.2% and damages far more than it 443

444 rescues -- a net loss of malignant cases. We report the two melanoma rates on 444

445 distinct conditional pools by construction: the salvage rate is over the salvageable 445

446 pool of melanomas already misclassified after degradation (n = 77), of which en- 446

447 hancement recovers 4 (4/77 = 5.2%), while the damage rate is over the at-risk 447

448 pool still correctly called after degradation (n = 274), of which enhancement 448

449 pushes 85 below the threshold (85/274 = 31.0%); the two denominators differ 449

450 because the events are conditioned on opposite pre-enhancement outcomes, not 450

451 because cases were selected. Across all 351 melanoma case¡severity instances (the 451

452 117 paired-subset melanomas each evaluated at the three severity tiers of Sec- 452

453 tion 6) this leaves a net change of 4 - 85 = -81 correctly-called malignant cases. 453

454 Because the benign subgroup dominates the population, the benign-dominated 454

455 aggregate moderate-band salvage rate (0.737) is therefore not a meaningful ca- 455

456 pability number, and we deliberately do not report it as one; only the per-class 456

457 breakdown reflects clinical risk. This is not a reconstruction-loss artefact: retrain- 457

458 ing with a mask-weighted objective (4Î lesion weight) leaves melanoma salvage 458

459 unchanged at 5.2% (an honest null; Fig. 2A), so the failure mode lies beyond 459

460 what per-pixel L1 can reach. The boundary is sharper still on the severe band, 460

461 where enhancement significantly lowers diagnostic AUC (AUC = -0.0559, 461

462 95% CI [-0.085, -0.028]). The agent accordingly routes severe and high-risk 462

463 inputs to query-for-retake rather than enhance, with the retake fraction rising 463

464 strictly with degradation across bands (Fig. 2C), realising the threshold-window 464

465 policy of Theorem 1. These per-class, per-severity, and per-dimension triggers 465

466 are the hardest motivation for the gate, but they are local, per-band statements 466

467 only; the honest global comparison is in Sec. 7.8.                                              467

489 8 Discussion and Limitations                                                    489

490 Relation to gradient-repair and generative restoration. As detailed in Sec. 2, 490

491 our contribution is orthogonal to GradProm: where GradProm repairs gradi- 491

492 ents during training, we derive a diagnosis-preserving information lower bound 492

493 (Lemma 1) and add a routing decision with a query-for-retake channel--complementa4r9y3,

494 not competing. We avoid diffusion- and GAN-based restoration because optimis- 494

495 ing perceptual realism can synthesise plausible-but-absent structure (pigment 495

496 networks, vessels), a medical-safety hazard; deterministic restoration instead 496

497 trades sharpness for an information-preservation property a generative model 497

498 cannot guarantee, and diffusion enters only as a rejected cautionary contrast.  498

499 What the method establishes, and its honest boundary. The positive contribution 499
500 is concrete and statistically attributable: within the moderate-degradation win- 500
501 dow the diagnosis-preserving objective lets QP-Enhanceretain the discriminative 501
502 information a frozen probe relies on (DP-Loss ablation AUCenh = +0.0205, 502
503 95% CI [+0.005, +0.035]; McNemar p = 2.3 Î 10-45), and it preserves diagnosis 503
504 better than six restoration baselines on every one under a matched zero-shot 504
505 protocol (the fine-tuned comparison is reported in Sec. 7.5). We are equally 505

506 explicit about where the method stops: we make no claim that quality-aware 506

507 triage beats direct diagnosis globally, since at a fixed threshold direct diagno- 507

508 sis attains the best auto-handled sensitivity (0.818 versus 0.788 for the strongest 508

509 variant at a 20% referral budget) with net-benefit CIs overlapping (Sec. 7.8). The 509

510 positive claim is local, and recoverability is dimension- and severity-dependent 510

512 Limitations and future work. Several limitations are themselves the motivation 512

513 for the retake gate, stated plainly. (1) The theory is argued under explicit as- 513

514 sumptions rather than watertight, and Theorem 1 is band-local, not a global 514

515 ordering. (2) Degradations are semi-synthetic; prospective validation on gen- 515

516 uinely degraded clinical captures remains open. (3) Routing depends on a per- 516

517 input quality scalar whose annotation and re-fitting cost we do not eliminate, 517

518 with claims restricted to dermatology. (4) We report no clinical deployment, and 518

519 diagnosis-preservation results are tied to a single frozen probe. (5) Enhancement 519

520 is uneven across axes, backfiring on contrast and underperforming on colour-shift 520

521 (Sec. 7.6). (6) The malignant-salvage gap is unresolved: enhancement salvages 521

522 only 5.2% of misclassified melanomas (a net loss of malignant cases), unchanged 522

523 by a mask-weighted retrain, and closing it is the central open question. These 523

524 negative results are genuine evidence that the retake gate is a necessary safety 524

525 mechanism, not a project shortcoming.                                        525

526 9 Conclusion                                                                 526

527 We presented a closed-loop quality-triage system for skin-lesion diagnosis on de- 527

528 graded consumer imagery, built on two contributions: a feature-level diagnosis- 528

529 preserving enhancement objective (DP-Loss) whose information loss is bounded 529

530 by a mutual-information lower bound (Lemma 1) and confirmed empirically, 530

531 and a query-for-retake channel that -- to our knowledge, the first to request 531

532 re-acquisition rather than merely abstain or defer -- closes the acquisition loop 532

533 under a local, per-band conditional risk guarantee (Theorem 1). We also chart its 533

534 limits: enhancement turns net-negative on extreme contrast loss and on malig- 534

535 nant lesions under severe degradation, and global triage does not yet beat direct 535

536 diagnosis -- honest negatives we read as motivation for learned routing thresh- 536

537 olds. Mapping where degradation-aware enhancement helps, and where it must 537

538 yield to re-acquisition, is the more useful contribution for deploying diagnosis on 538

539 the quality-varying inputs that curated benchmarks exclude by design.        539

1222 J LLM-as-Clinical-Judge Protocol                            1222

1223 J.1 Purpose and Scope                                       1223

1224 This protocol provides an audit signal for qualitative properties that scalar met- 1224
1225 rics cannot capture -- in particular, whether enhancement introduces implausi- 1225
1226 ble structure and whether the agent's routing decision is reasonable on a case. It 1226
1227 is explicitly not a reader study and not a substitute for clinician evaluation; its 1227
1228 outputs are indicative and are interpreted under the disclaimer in Appendix M. 1228

1229 J.2 Case Selection                                          1229

1230 We draw a held-out set of 200 cases, stratified to cover (i) the four quality strata 1230

1231 of QSB, (ii) the four agent channels (direct, cautioned, enhance-then-diagnose, 1231

1232 query-for-retake), and (iii) both outcome types (correct and confidently-wrong), 1232

1233 so that the audit is not dominated by easy cases. Stratum sizes are fixed in 1233

1234 advance and released with the benchmark.                    1234

1235 J.3 Judge Panel                                             1235

1236 Three frontier large language models from distinct providers serve as indepen- 1236

1237 dent judges, each prompted to adopt a dermatology-reviewer persona with an 1237

1238 identical, fixed rubric. Using heterogeneous models reduces single-model bias; 1238

1239 we report per-judge and aggregated scores. Judges see the (anonymised) image, 1239

1240 the model's prediction and uncertainty, and -- for enhancement cases -- the 1240

1241 reference and enhanced images side by side.                 1241

1242 J.4 Rubric                                                  1242

1243 Each case is scored on a 1¡5 Likert scale along four axes:  1243

1244 1. Diagnostic plausibility -- is the prediction consistent with visible fea- 1244

1245      tures?                                                 1245

1246 2. Artefact introduction -- did enhancement add structure absent from the 1246

1247      reference? (reverse-scored: 5 = no new structure)      1247

1248 3. Enhancement faithfulness -- are restored details consistent with the de- 1248

1249      graded input?                                          1249

1250 4. Triage appropriateness -- was the agent's channel choice reasonable for 1250

1251      this case?                                             1251

1252 The rubric text, persona prompt, and scoring anchors are released verbatim for 1252

1253 reproducibility.                                            1253
                                      ACCV 2026 Submission #*****                41

1254 J.5 Reliability and Aggregation                                                 1254

1255 Inter-judge agreement is quantified with Cohen's  (pairwise) and Fleiss'  1255

1256 (panel); we set a pre-registered acceptance floor of  > 0.5 (moderate agree- 1256

1257 ment) below which the axis is reported as inconclusive rather than aggregated. 1257

1258 Case-level scores are aggregated by the panel mean, with majority vote as a 1258

1259 robustness check.                                                               1259

1260 J.6 Analysis                                                                    1260

1261 We test whether judge scores align with the model's own uncertainty -- e.g. 1261

1262 whether low diagnostic-plausibility scores concentrate on high-entropy predic- 1262

1263 tions, and whether artefact-introduction scores are uncorrelated with q» improve- 1263

1264 ment (the desired behaviour for a diagnosis-preserving map). Results are re- 1264

1265 ported in Sec. 7.8 once the panel run completes.                                1265

1267 An LLM judge is not a clinician: it can share blind spots across models, is sensi- 1267

1268 tive to prompt framing, and cannot access clinical context beyond the image and 1268

1269 metadata provided. We therefore use it only as a complementary audit along- 1269

1270 side decision-curve analysis and triage simulation, never as evidence of clinical 1270

1271 validity (Appendix M).                                                          1271

1272 K Cost¡Benefit Deployment Analysis                                              1272

1273 K.1 Motivation                                                                  1273

1274 A quality-triage system trades one cost (retakes and specialist referrals) against 1274

1275 another (missed or mis-staged lesions). Whether this trade is favourable cannot 1275

1276 be read off accuracy alone; it depends on the relative costs of each error and ac- 1276

1277 tion. The stakes on the error side are large: the annual cost-of-illness of melanoma 1277

1278 in Europe has been estimated at approximately EUR 2.7 billion per year [17], 1278

1279 and the dominant driver of per-patient lifetime cost is late staging rather than 1279

1280 the diagnostic procedure itself. The delay-amplification effect is illustrated by 1280

1281 the estimated $7.65 billion (95% CI $6.89¡8.44 billion) of additional costs at- 1281

1282 tributed to COVID-19-related delays in melanoma diagnosis across 31 European 1282

1283 countries [22]; we cite this figure only as evidence that diagnostic delay magni- 1283

1284 fies cost and emphasise that it is a pandemic-scenario cumulative excess, not a 1284

1285 steady-state annual burden. This appendix specifies the decision-analytic model 1285

1286 used to assess the quality-triage trade; the numerical instantiation (simulated 1286

1287 operating curves and net-benefit point estimates) is reported in Sec. 7.8.      1287

1511 re-run or approximate these baselines; they are external anchors, and we note 1511
