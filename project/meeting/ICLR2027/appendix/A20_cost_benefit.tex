\section{Cost--Benefit Deployment Analysis}\label{app:a20}

\subsection{Motivation}\label{app:a20-motiv}
A quality-triage system trades one cost (retakes and specialist referrals) against another (missed or
mis-staged lesions). Whether this trade is favourable cannot be read off accuracy alone; it depends on
the relative costs of each error and action. The stakes on the error side are large: the annual
cost-of-illness of melanoma in Europe has been estimated at approximately EUR~2.7 billion per year
\citep{krensel2019melanomacost}, and the dominant driver of per-patient lifetime cost is late staging
rather than the diagnostic procedure itself. The delay-amplification effect is illustrated by the
estimated \$7.65 billion (95\% CI \$6.89--8.44 billion) of additional costs attributed to
\emph{COVID-19-related} delays in melanoma diagnosis across 31 European countries
\citep{maul2024melanomacovid}; we cite this figure only as evidence that diagnostic delay magnifies cost
and emphasise that it is a pandemic-scenario cumulative excess, not a steady-state annual burden. This
appendix specifies the decision-analytic model used to assess the quality-triage trade; the numerical
instantiation (simulated operating curves and net-benefit point estimates) is reported in
\cref{sec:exp-dca}.

\subsection{Cost Model}\label{app:a20-model}
For a case routed to action $a\in\{\actd,\acte,\actq,\actr\}$ with true label $y$ and prediction
$\hat{y}$, we assign an expected cost
\begin{equation}
C(a,y,\hat{y}) = c_{\mathrm{err}}(y,\hat{y}) + c_{\mathrm{act}}(a),
\label{eq:a20-cost}
\end{equation}
where $c_{\mathrm{err}}$ is the diagnostic-error cost (a false negative on a malignant lesion
dominates, reflecting downstream morbidity, optionally expressed in DALY/QALY units) and
$c_{\mathrm{act}}$ is the operational cost of the action (negligible for $\actd$; a capture cost for
$\actq$; a specialist-time cost for $\actr$; an inference/enhancement cost for $\acte$). All cost
constants are exposed as parameters and swept rather than fixed to a single jurisdiction.

\paragraph{Literature-anchored cost constants.}
Rather than fixing a single point estimate, we anchor each constant to a value reported in the
peer-reviewed health-economic literature and treat that value as the centre of a sensitivity range; the
constants are summarised in \cref{tab:a20-costs}. Several caveats on calibre apply and are reproduced
in the table so that no figure is read out of context: the biopsy cost is the 2020 cross-CPT weighted
average paid amount for dermatologist-performed skin biopsies in the US Medicare Part~B
fee-for-service population, not a single current CPT rate \citep{li2023skinbiopsy}; the missed-diagnosis
cost difference is a UK NHS per-patient \emph{cumulative lifetime} estimate contrasting Stage~I with
Stage~IV melanoma \citep{mistry2026melanomacost}; and the realtime-teledermatology marginal cost is
expressed in 2001 GBP \citep{loane2001teledermatology}, complemented by a more recent estimate that
community teledermatology saves on average \pounds45 per case (95\% CI \pounds22--60, 2022--23 prices)
relative to conventional pathways \citep{hoogenboom2026teledermatology}. Because these values span
different jurisdictions, currencies, and base years, we never combine them into a single absolute
monetary verdict; instead each enters \cref{eq:a20-breakeven} as a swept parameter, and the reported
analysis is a sensitivity study over the resulting ranges.

\paragraph{Break-even condition.}
Combining the error and action terms, quality-triage is economically favourable precisely when the
savings from reduced missed diagnoses and from avoided unnecessary biopsies exceed the marginal
operating cost of the triage system. Writing $\pi$ for the disease prevalence, $\Delta\mathrm{sens}$ for
the sensitivity gain attributable to triage, $\Delta r_{\mathrm{avoid}}$ for the change in the rate of
avoided unnecessary biopsies, and $c_{\mathrm{sys}}$ for the marginal system operating cost, the
condition is
\begin{equation}
\pi\,c_{\mathrm{miss}}\,\Delta\mathrm{sens}
\;+\;
(1-\pi)\,(c_{\mathrm{biopsy}}-c_{\mathrm{refer}})\,\Delta r_{\mathrm{avoid}}
\;>\;
c_{\mathrm{sys}},
\label{eq:a20-breakeven}
\end{equation}
where $c_{\mathrm{miss}}$ is the downstream cost difference of a missed malignant lesion (the
Stage~I$\to$IV gap of \pounds204{,}289 above), $c_{\mathrm{biopsy}}$ the biopsy cost, $c_{\mathrm{refer}}$
the teledermatology referral marginal cost, and the four-action notation
$\{\actd,\acte,\actq,\actr\}$ maps the routing decisions of the closed-loop policy onto these terms: a
correct $\actr$ (query-for-retake or referral) on a degraded malignant case realises the
$c_{\mathrm{miss}}$ saving, whereas an unnecessary $\actr$ on a benign case incurs only the
comparatively small $(c_{\mathrm{refer}})$ term in place of $c_{\mathrm{biopsy}}$.
Inequality~\eqref{eq:a20-breakeven} is an analytic condition, not an empirical result: it identifies
\emph{which} regions of the cost--prevalence space favour triage. We instantiate the corresponding
numerical analysis as a sensitivity study in \cref{app:a20-netbenefit,app:a20-sweep}, computed entirely
from held-out \itb{}-LQ predictions ($n=300$) rather than asserted from the literature-anchored
constants tabulated above; the constants enter only as the swept centres of the cost-ratio range.

\subsection{Net Benefit and Decision Curves}\label{app:a20-netbenefit}
We report \emph{net benefit} as a function of the decision threshold $p_t$,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{NB}(p_t) = \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{N} - \frac{\mathrm{FP}}{N}\cdot\frac{p_t}{1-p_t},
\label{eq:a20-nb}
\end{equation}
and compare the information-bottleneck variants against (i) treat-all, (ii) treat-none, and (iii)
direct diagnosis without triage. The decision curves and the confidence-gated triage operating points
are summarised in the main text (\cref{sec:exp-dca}, Fig.~\ref{fig:dca}); here we record the supporting
point estimates with their $2{,}000$-sample bootstrap $95\%$ confidence intervals, computed entirely
from held-out \itb{}-LQ predictions ($n=300$) and requiring no human readers.

\paragraph{Maximum net benefit.}
Over the clinically relevant threshold range the maximum net benefit is \emph{statistically
indistinguishable} across direct diagnosis and all information-bottleneck variants: EfficientNet-B3
(direct) $0.186$ ($95\%$ CI $[0.143,0.233]$), \qvib{}$+$TokFT $0.179$ $[0.139,0.223]$, Std VIB $0.186$
$[0.142,0.233]$, and Std VIB${}+{}$QCTS $0.192$ $[0.148,0.236]$. All four confidence intervals overlap,
so the decision curves do not separate the methods and \textbf{we make no claim that
quality-conditional enhancement raises net benefit over direct diagnosis}. The single positive
statement the analysis supports is that every method attains net benefit above zero --- i.e.\ each is no
worse than the treat-all reference --- across the threshold range examined.

\paragraph{Confidence-gated triage.}
The clinical value surfaces instead under confidence-gated triage, where low-confidence cases are
referred to a human reader. At a $20\%$ referral budget the well-calibrated \qvib{}$+$TokFT
\emph{matches} direct diagnosis on auto-handled sensitivity ($0.788$ versus $0.818$; missed-positive
rate $0.183$ versus $0.167$), whereas the under-confident Std VIB and Std VIB${}+{}$QCTS variants
collapse at their natural operating thresholds (auto-handled sensitivity $0.137$ and $0.143$, missing
$73\%$ and $70\%$ of positives respectively) because their compressed posteriors rarely cross the
referral threshold. The operative finding is therefore calibration-driven reliable abstention rather
than a raw-discrimination gain.

\subsection{Cost-Ratio Sensitivity Sweep}\label{app:a20-sweep}
On top of the static cost model we sweep the single dimensionless quantity that drives the trade-off,
the miss-to-biopsy cost ratio $r = c_{\mathrm{miss}}/c_{\mathrm{biopsy}}$, over the range spanned by the
literature-anchored constants of \cref{tab:a20-costs}, and report two complementary quantities computed
from the held-out \itb{}-LQ predictions ($n=300$).

\paragraph{Expected cost is the cost-sensitive axis.}
The normalised expected cost of a decision rule,
\begin{equation}
\widehat{C}(r) \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{FP}+\mathrm{TP}}{n} \;+\; \frac{\mathrm{FN}}{n}\cdot r,
\label{eq:a20-expcost}
\end{equation}
charges every positive decision a unit biopsy cost and every false negative the relative miss cost $r$.
By construction $\widehat{C}(r)$ is monotonically increasing in $r$ (verifier-confirmed on the swept
grid), so it is the quantity on which the break-even of \cref{eq:a20-breakeven} actually plays out: as
the miss cost rises relative to the biopsy cost, rules that abstain on uncertain malignant cases (and
thus avoid the heavily-weighted $\mathrm{FN}$ term) become progressively favoured. The break-even
between any two rules is therefore located on the expected-cost axis, not the net-benefit axis.

\paragraph{Net benefit is, by definition, invariant to $r$.}
The net benefit of \cref{eq:a20-nb} is parametrised by the decision threshold $p_t$ alone and does not
contain $r$; consequently the maximum net benefit reported in \cref{app:a20-netbenefit} is invariant
under the cost-ratio sweep (this is a property of the standard DCA definition, not an empirical finding,
and we state it explicitly to avoid the misreading that the overlapping net-benefit intervals are
themselves cost-insensitive results). The two analyses are complementary: net benefit fixes the
threshold-implied cost ratio internally, whereas \eqref{eq:a20-expcost} exposes $r$ as an explicit
swept parameter.

\paragraph{What the sweep does and does not establish.}
Across the swept cost-ratio range every method retains positive net benefit (no worse than treat-all),
and the four maximum-net-benefit intervals remain mutually overlapping throughout; we therefore again
make \textbf{no} claim that enhancement raises net benefit. The defensible positive reading is the
triage one: a well-calibrated posterior (\qvib{}$+$TokFT) supports confidence-ordered abstention whose
auto-handled sensitivity ($0.788$) tracks direct diagnosis ($0.818$), whereas the under-confident bare
Std VIB misses $73\%$ of positives at its natural operating point and so cannot be gated for safe
abstention at all. The sweep inherits the safety boundary of \cref{sec:exp-salvage} (a low salvage rate
on severely degraded inputs is intended, not a failure) and is presented as a deployment-feasibility
sensitivity study rather than a health-economic verdict.

\subsection{Reporting and Limitations}\label{app:a20-limits}
We report the cost model as a \emph{sensitivity analysis} over the cost constants and the
miss-to-biopsy cost ratio rather than as a single point estimate, since real costs vary by health
system. Three caveats bound the reading. First, the four maximum-net-benefit confidence intervals
overlap, so we make no claim that quality-conditional enhancement raises net benefit over direct
diagnosis; the only net-benefit statements we assert are that each method is no worse than treat-all and
that calibration enables reliable abstention. Second, the cost constants of \cref{tab:a20-costs} are
swept parameters anchored to peer-reviewed estimates, never point estimates, and are not converted to a
common unit. Third, the analysis is a deployment-feasibility argument, not a health-economic evaluation;
a formal cost-effectiveness study with jurisdiction-specific inputs is out of scope and listed as future
work in \cref{sec:discussion}.
