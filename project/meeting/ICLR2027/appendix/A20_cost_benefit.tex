\section{Cost--Benefit Deployment Analysis}\label{app:a20}

\subsection{Motivation}\label{app:a20-motiv}
A quality-triage system trades one cost (retakes and specialist referrals) against another (missed or
mis-staged lesions). Whether this trade is favourable cannot be read off accuracy alone; it depends on
the relative costs of each error and action. The stakes on the error side are large: the annual
cost-of-illness of melanoma in Europe has been estimated at approximately EUR~2.7 billion per year
\citep{krensel2019melanomacost}, and the dominant driver of per-patient lifetime cost is late staging
rather than the diagnostic procedure itself. The delay-amplification effect is illustrated by the
estimated \$7.65 billion (95\% CI \$6.89--8.44 billion) of additional costs attributed to
\emph{COVID-19-related} delays in melanoma diagnosis across 31 European countries
\citep{maul2024melanomacovid}; we cite this figure only as evidence that diagnostic delay magnifies cost
and emphasise that it is a pandemic-scenario cumulative excess, not a steady-state annual burden. This
appendix specifies the decision-analytic model used to assess the quality-triage trade; the numerical
instantiation (simulated operating curves and net-benefit point estimates) is reported in
\cref{sec:exp-dca}.

\subsection{Cost Model}\label{app:a20-model}
For a case routed to action $a\in\{\actd,\acte,\actq,\actr\}$ with true label $y$ and prediction
$\hat{y}$, we assign an expected cost
\begin{equation}
C(a,y,\hat{y}) = c_{\mathrm{err}}(y,\hat{y}) + c_{\mathrm{act}}(a),
\label{eq:a20-cost}
\end{equation}
where $c_{\mathrm{err}}$ is the diagnostic-error cost (a false negative on a malignant lesion
dominates, reflecting downstream morbidity, optionally expressed in DALY/QALY units) and
$c_{\mathrm{act}}$ is the operational cost of the action (negligible for $\actd$; a capture cost for
$\actq$; a specialist-time cost for $\actr$; an inference/enhancement cost for $\acte$). All cost
constants are exposed as parameters and swept rather than fixed to a single jurisdiction.

\paragraph{Literature-anchored cost constants.}
Rather than fixing a single point estimate, we anchor each constant to a value reported in the
peer-reviewed health-economic literature and treat that value as the centre of a sensitivity range; the
constants are summarised in \cref{tab:a20-costs}. Several caveats on calibre apply and are reproduced
in the table so that no figure is read out of context: the biopsy cost is the 2020 cross-CPT weighted
average paid amount for dermatologist-performed skin biopsies in the US Medicare Part~B
fee-for-service population, not a single current CPT rate \citep{li2023skinbiopsy}; the missed-diagnosis
cost difference is a UK NHS per-patient \emph{cumulative lifetime} estimate contrasting Stage~I with
Stage~IV melanoma \citep{mistry2026melanomacost}; and the realtime-teledermatology marginal cost is
expressed in 2001 GBP \citep{loane2001teledermatology}, complemented by a more recent estimate that
community teledermatology saves on average \pounds45 per case (95\% CI \pounds22--60, 2022--23 prices)
relative to conventional pathways \citep{hoogenboom2026teledermatology}. Because these values span
different jurisdictions, currencies, and base years, we never combine them into a single absolute
monetary verdict; instead each enters \cref{eq:a20-breakeven} as a swept parameter, and the reported
analysis is a sensitivity study over the resulting ranges.

\paragraph{Break-even condition.}
Combining the error and action terms, quality-triage is economically favourable precisely when the
savings from reduced missed diagnoses and from avoided unnecessary biopsies exceed the marginal
operating cost of the triage system. Writing $\pi$ for the disease prevalence, $\Delta\mathrm{sens}$ for
the sensitivity gain attributable to triage, $\Delta r_{\mathrm{avoid}}$ for the change in the rate of
avoided unnecessary biopsies, and $c_{\mathrm{sys}}$ for the marginal system operating cost, the
condition is
\begin{equation}
\pi\,c_{\mathrm{miss}}\,\Delta\mathrm{sens}
\;+\;
(1-\pi)\,(c_{\mathrm{biopsy}}-c_{\mathrm{refer}})\,\Delta r_{\mathrm{avoid}}
\;>\;
c_{\mathrm{sys}},
\label{eq:a20-breakeven}
\end{equation}
where $c_{\mathrm{miss}}$ is the downstream cost difference of a missed malignant lesion (the
Stage~I$\to$IV gap of \pounds204{,}289 above), $c_{\mathrm{biopsy}}$ the biopsy cost, $c_{\mathrm{refer}}$
the teledermatology referral marginal cost, and the four-action notation
$\{\actd,\acte,\actq,\actr\}$ maps the routing decisions of the closed-loop policy onto these terms: a
correct $\actr$ (query-for-retake or referral) on a degraded malignant case realises the
$c_{\mathrm{miss}}$ saving, whereas an unnecessary $\actr$ on a benign case incurs only the
comparatively small $(c_{\mathrm{refer}})$ term in place of $c_{\mathrm{biopsy}}$.
Inequality~\eqref{eq:a20-breakeven} is an analytic condition, not an empirical result; it identifies
\emph{which} regions of the cost--prevalence space favour triage, and the corresponding numerical
operating curves remain pending and unreported here (\cref{sec:exp-dca}, deferred to M3), since they
require a real simulation run rather than the literature-anchored constants tabulated above.

\subsection{Net Benefit and Decision Curves}\label{app:a20-netbenefit}
We report \emph{net benefit} as a function of the decision threshold $p_t$,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{NB}(p_t) = \frac{\mathrm{TP}}{N} - \frac{\mathrm{FP}}{N}\cdot\frac{p_t}{1-p_t},
\label{eq:a20-nb}
\end{equation}
and compare the closed-loop agent against (i) treat-all, (ii) treat-none, and (iii) direct diagnosis
without triage. Decision-curve dominance over a clinically relevant $p_t$ range is the headline
deployment claim; it requires no human readers and is computed entirely from held-out predictions.

\subsection{Triage Simulation}\label{app:a20-sim}
On top of the static cost model we simulate the closed loop: degraded inputs are routed by the policy
of Theorem~\ref{thm:agent}, retakes draw a higher-quality re-capture from the paired data,
enhancement applies $\Tw$, and referrals remove the case from automated decision. Sweeping the
quality thresholds $(\tauenh,\tauhigh)$ traces an operating curve of expected cost versus referral
rate, identifying the band where triage is economically favourable. The simulation inherits the
safety boundary of \cref{sec:exp-salvage} (low salvage rate on severely degraded inputs is intended,
not a failure).

\subsection{Reporting and Limitations}\label{app:a20-limits}
We report the cost model as a \emph{sensitivity analysis} over the cost constants rather than a single
point estimate, since real costs vary by health system. The analysis is a deployment-feasibility
argument, not a health-economic evaluation; a formal cost-effectiveness study with jurisdiction-specific
inputs is out of scope and listed as future work in \cref{sec:discussion}.
