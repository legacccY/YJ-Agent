## REVIEW 1 — Senior BMVC Area Chair

**Persona:** 12 years on program committees, has seen every incremental calibration paper since Guo et al. 2017. Deeply allergic to re-labelling old ideas. Signal-to-noise ratio of reviewers' time is their primary lens.

**Overall Recommendation: Weak Reject**

**Confidence: High**

### Summary

The paper identifies a real failure mode (quality-oblivious calibration) and proposes a two-parameter fix (QCTS). The core observation — that temperature scaling can *reverse* the entropy-quality correlation on weakly quality-aware backbones — is genuinely interesting. However, the paper packages a one-parameter extension of temperature scaling as a benchmark contribution + a method contribution + a theoretical contribution, and none of the three delivers at BMVC standard. The benchmark is a partitioned subset of existing public datasets. The method is T(q) = softplus(T0 + α(1−q̄)) with two scalars. The PAC-Bayes "sketch" in §4.3 is not a sketch — it is informal hand-waving that would be rejected by any theory track. I am not yet convinced this is more than a solid workshop paper.

### Top Criticisms

**C1 — The method contribution is weaker than claimed (§4.2, §5.3)**

QCTS has exactly one free parameter over standard TS: the slope α. The paper frames this as "two-parameter," but T0 is already present in standard TS (it is just the log of the temperature). The net novelty is a single multiplicative slope that linearly scales temperature with (1 − q̄). Platt scaling with a quality-stratified feature is strictly more expressive, yet is never compared. Isotonic regression on quality-binned data (a non-parametric monotone calibrator) has been in the calibration toolkit since 2002 (Zadrozny & Elkan). Neither appears in Table 2 (ablation) or as a baseline. The authors need to demonstrate that QCTS outperforms these obvious competitors, not just alternative functional forms of the same softplus family.

**C2 — Benchmark novelty is overstated (§4.1)**

ITB is four public datasets (ISIC 2020, FitzPatrick17k, HAM10000, PAD-UFES) stratified by a learned quality scalar. The authors call this a "benchmark," but it is a partitioning methodology applied to existing data, not a new corpus. The 149,100-image IQA training pool used to produce q̄ is generated by synthetic degradation of the same public images — there is no new annotation effort. The claim that "no existing benchmark systematically interrogates the relationship between image quality and calibration quality" (§1) is technically true but somewhat tautological: once you define a quality scalar and partition on it, any dataset becomes a quality-stratified benchmark. The authors should position ITB as an evaluation *protocol* rather than a *benchmark*, and acknowledge that the quality labels are synthetic constructs, not ground-truth human quality judgements.

**C3 — The QCDI taxonomy thresholds are arbitrary and uncalibrated (§3.2–3.3)**

The paper defines Quality-Oblivious as QCDI > 0.10, Quality-Fragile as 0 < QCDI ≤ 0.10, Quality-Aware as QCDI ≤ 0. These thresholds are nowhere motivated. Why 0.10? The paper notes that MC Dropout achieves QCDI ≈ 0.124 on ITB-Diverse (fairness data; §5.6), not dramatically above the oblivious threshold. More critically, QCDI is defined as ECE_LQ − ECE_HQ, so it conflates two sources of variation: (i) genuine quality-induced miscalibration and (ii) floor effects when both ECE_LQ and ECE_HQ are already small. QCTS achieves QCDI = +0.004 on Std VIB largely because it reduces ECE_LQ from 0.079 to something near ECE_HQ = 0.075 — a genuine improvement, but one that is not robustly distinguished from QCDI = 0 given the n = 300 sample size. A confidence interval on QCDI is never reported.

**C4 — α variance across seeds is alarming and underreported (§5.3)**

The paper reports three seeds converge to α = 0.34, 0.96, 0.37. This is a factor-of-2.8 spread across an identical training protocol. The "best run" at α = 0.96 is presented without acknowledging that this single seed drives the headline results. All claims about T(q̄ = 0) ≈ 2.24 versus T(q̄ = 1) ≈ 1.44 are derived from the best seed, not the median. The main table presumably aggregates over seeds for ECE (it says "averaged over three training seeds"), but Fig. 3(a) shows the 16/84% band. The reader cannot tell which numbers in Table 1 correspond to which seed-aggregation strategy for the headline QCDI = +0.004 vs. +0.015 comparison. This is a reproducibility red flag.

**C5 — The TS reversal claim is the strongest finding, but the structural explanation is not falsifiable (§6 Discussion)**

The paper attributes reversal to "backbones whose raw quality-awareness is weak but whose logit magnitudes are dominated by a quality-independent trend." This is a post-hoc narrative. The paper does not provide a falsifiable criterion for predicting *in advance* whether a new backbone will invert. Without such a criterion, the taxonomy (quality-aware / fragile / oblivious) cannot serve as a diagnostic tool — a practitioner cannot know before running experiments whether TS will harm their model. The "structural reason" section (§6) should either derive a testable predictor (e.g., a threshold on |raw ρ|) or be reframed as a hypothesis rather than an explanation.

**C6 — The PAC-Bayes bound (Eq. 5) is not a bound (§4.3)**

Eq. 5 is introduced as a "generalisation sketch" but is presented in equation form with formal notation, creating the appearance of a theoretical contribution. However:
- The bound applies to expected ECE, but ECE is not a standard PAC-Bayes loss — it is a post-hoc statistic on binned predictions. The KL divergence in the RHS refers to a "Posterior_q" that is never defined in relation to the model's actual predictive distribution.
- The authors themselves write "we treat Eq. 5 as a motivating sketch rather than a tight bound." If the authors know it is not a bound, it should not appear in equation form with a formal citation to McAllester 1999.
- Either prove a proper PAC-Bayes calibration bound (which would be genuinely novel) or remove the equation and replace with intuition in prose.

**C7 — Missing critical baselines: conformal prediction and dirichlet calibration (§5.2)**

The paper concludes that MC Dropout and Deep Ensembles are "quality-oblivious." But two of the strongest modern calibration methods are absent: (i) temperature scaling with dirichlet calibration (Kull et al., NeurIPS 2019), which calibrates per-class and may be more expressive than scalar temperature; (ii) conformal prediction (Angelopoulos & Bates, 2023), which provides marginal coverage guarantees and can be applied quality-conditionally. The paper's conclusion in §7 mentions "conformal prediction" as a future direction — but if conformal already solves the quality-aware coverage problem, QCTS may not be needed. This possibility must be addressed.

**C8 — Teaser figure (Fig. 1) uses a synthetic setting that is not representative of ITB-LQ**

The teaser shows Std VIB assigning 81–96% melanoma probability to benign lesions under degradation. However, the ITB-LQ pool is constructed from programmatic degradation of ISIC 2020 images — the same images the backbone trained on. A model that assigns 81–96% to a benign lesion likely has a pre-existing classification error, and the degradation is incidental. The figure does not control for whether the high-confidence wrong prediction exists on the clean version of the same image. If Std VIB already misclassifies the clean version at 79%, degradation pushing it to 81% is not a calibration failure attributable to image quality.

### Required Changes

1. Add Platt scaling (quality-stratified) and isotonic regression as baselines in Table 2.
2. Report confidence intervals on QCDI, not point estimates.
3. Report all three seed results separately for the headline QCDI comparison, not just "averaged."
4. Remove or replace the PAC-Bayes equation with honest prose.
5. Provide a testable criterion for predicting reversal on unseen backbones.

### Minor Suggestions

- Rename ITB from "benchmark" to "evaluation protocol" or "stratified evaluation suite."
- Clarify in the abstract that the main method is a one-parameter extension over TS.

### Killer Question

*The three fitted α values across seeds are 0.34, 0.96, and 0.37 — a 2.8× range. If the method's key hyperparameter is this unstable, what guarantees that a practitioner replicating your results on a new dataset will land in the same regime? And if they can't know in advance whether α ≈ 0.34 or α ≈ 0.96, how does QCTS differ from running standard TS (α = 0) and hoping for the best?*

## REVIEW 2 — Non-Domain CV Reviewer (Unfamiliar with Medical AI)

**Persona:** Strong ICCV/CVPR background in self-supervised learning and domain generalisation. First medical AI paper on their stack this cycle. Will judge the paper on whether the contributions generalise beyond the specific experimental setup.

**Overall Recommendation: Weak Reject**

**Confidence: Medium**

### Summary

This paper studies whether model confidence is appropriately lower on low-quality images than on high-quality images — a sensible goal. The proposed fix (QCTS) conditions temperature scaling on a per-image quality score. The experiments are carefully done on dermoscopy data. But the paper makes BMVC-level claims (new benchmark, new taxonomy, new method) while every component is tightly coupled to a single domain, a single quality metric (the 5-head IQA module), and a specific disease classification task. As a general-audience CV paper, it does not demonstrate that the problem formulation or the solution transfers to settings that do not share these domain-specific assumptions.

### Top Criticisms

**C1 — Why build a new benchmark when CIFAR-10-C, ImageNet-C, and Camelyon17 already exist? (§1, §4.1)**

The paper argues in §1 that "no existing benchmark systematically evaluates how image quality affects calibration quality." But CIFAR-10-C and ImageNet-C do exactly this: they define quality via corruption severity at five levels and have been used to benchmark calibration since Ovadia et al. 2019. Camelyon17 (WILDS) provides hospital-shift distribution shift in histopathology — a medical imaging analogue. The paper's response is that existing benchmarks don't use a *domain-specific learned quality scalar*. But §5.5 of this very paper shows that QCTS works equally well when q̄ is operationalised as ImageNet-C corruption severity (a non-learned scalar). If the method transfers to non-learned quality proxies, the necessity of the 5-head IQA module and the ITB construction is undermined. The paper has an internal contradiction: the method works on ImageNet-C-style scalars, but the benchmark is justified on the grounds that domain-specific quality labels are needed.

**C2 — The 5-head IQA module is an unreviewed contribution buried in Appendix A (§3.1)**

The entire evaluation depends on the quality scalar q̄, which is produced by a "5-head IQA module trained on synthetic degradations." But:
- Appendix A is referenced but not reproduced in this submission (the paper says "training details are deferred to Appendix A").
- Reviewers cannot evaluate the validity of the quality labels without seeing the IQA architecture, training data, and validation procedure.
- If the IQA module has systematic biases (e.g., it conflates image brightness with lesion-level quality, or consistently mislabels dark-skinned patients as lower quality), the entire ITB partition and the QCDI metric are invalid.
- The ablation in §5.3 shows BRISQUE and CLIP-IQA "collapse" to quality-agnostic solutions (α ≈ 0), but does not show that the 5-head IQA's quality scores correlate with human quality judgements, dermatologist assessments, or clinical usability ratings. The IQA module's validity is assumed, not demonstrated.

**C3 — Generalisability beyond dermoscopy is asserted, not demonstrated (§5.5–5.6)**

The paper has a "Cross-modality check" (§5.6) on chest X-ray (CheXpert) and fundus (APTOS 2019). The CheXpert result shows ρ = −0.971 — extremely strong quality-awareness — and no TS reversal, which the paper explains is "consistent with our hypothesis that TS reversal is specific to weakly quality-aware backbones." But this is circular: the paper selects settings where the hypothesis predicts no reversal, observes no reversal, and calls it a confirmation. The test would be falsifiable only if the paper had a setting where reversal was *predicted* to occur in a non-dermoscopy domain and then measured. The fundus result shows QCTS fails entirely (QCDI unchanged), which the paper explains as "the dermoscopy-trained 5-head IQA module is not calibrated to fundus degradation patterns." This is an important scope condition, but it means QCTS requires a new domain-specific IQA module for every new modality — substantially reducing the claimed generality.

**C4 — The baseline ECE numbers for MC Dropout and Deep Ensemble (0.615, 0.440) are not reproducible from any public implementation (§5.2)**

The paper reports MC Dropout ECE of 0.615 on ITB-LQ. This is an unusually high number — standard MC Dropout on dermoscopy typically achieves ECE in the range 0.05–0.15 on clean data (Leibig et al. 2017, Roy et al. 2019). The authors attribute the high ECE to poor quality-conditioned calibration, but it is not clear whether the ECE is measured *only* on the 300 ITB-LQ images (a very small slice) or the full pool. The paper says ECE is averaged over three seeds with n = 300 for ITB-LQ. With only 300 samples, ECE estimated with M = 15 bins has ~20 samples per bin on average — far too few for stable calibration curve estimation. The authors report a power analysis for the *mean* ECE comparison between QCTS and Std VIB (§5.3, power = 92.9%), but this analysis assumes a known effect size (Cohen's d = 0.45) that itself comes from the data. The power analysis is post-hoc and circular.

**C5 — The "Simpson's paradox" framing (§5.2) is overblown**

The paper reports that MC Dropout appears marginally quality-aware in aggregate (ρ ≈ −0.11 on the full ITB pool) but quality-oblivious within the critical LQ tier (ρ = +0.350 on n = 300). This is framed as a "Simpson-style divergence." But this is simply a consequence of the partitioning: the full-pool ρ is dominated by the HQ stratum where the correlation is correctly negative, while the LQ stratum shows positive ρ. This is not Simpson's paradox (which requires a reversal of direction driven by a confounding variable causing an apparent relationship to disappear). It is a stratification effect that any competent data analyst would expect when evaluating a model on a non-random subset. The framing inflates the apparent novelty of the finding.

**C6 — The claim that QCTS "transfers from EfficientNet to ResNet-50 and ViT-Tiny" (abstract) is misleading**

The transfer claim means that QCTS is re-fitted (two new parameters estimated) on each new backbone's validation set. This is not transfer learning — it is re-calibration. Every post-hoc calibration method "transfers" in this sense; standard TS also re-fits T on each backbone. The abstract implies a stronger claim than the paper delivers.

**C7 — Evaluation is single-disease (melanoma vs. benign) but dermatology has many classes (§4.1)**

The ITB binary classification task (melanoma/benign from ISIC 2020) is the simplest possible formulation of the clinical problem. Real dermatology AI systems must distinguish among 7–26 disease classes (Rotemberg et al. 2021 ISIC 2020 has 9 categories). The paper's calibration analysis on a binary problem does not generalise straightforwardly: ECE in a 9-class problem has different semantics, quality-conditioning may interact differently with class-wise calibration, and QCDI would need to be re-defined for multi-class ECE. This is not mentioned as a limitation.

**C8 — Section 5.6 reports V–VI QCDI based on n = 41 LQ images**

The paper warns in §5.6 that "the V–VI LQ stratum contains only n = 41 images, so QCDI estimates are noisy." But then reports QCDI = +0.014 for Std VIB on V–VI and QCDI ≈ 0.01–0.02 after QCTS, and uses this to conclude that "QCTS keeps the V–VI QCDI in the 0.01–0.02 range without introducing new bias on lighter skin types." With n = 41 LQ images, the confidence interval on QCDI is so wide that this conclusion is statistically meaningless. The fairness analysis cannot be treated as evidence with these sample sizes.

### Required Changes

1. Reproduce Appendix A in the main paper or supplementary (IQA module architecture, training procedure, validation against human quality judgements).
2. Compute confidence intervals on all ECE and QCDI values with n = 300.
3. Either drop the "benchmark" claim and reframe ITB as an evaluation protocol, or add a genuinely new dataset (real-world, human-annotated quality labels).
4. Address the internal contradiction: if QCTS works with ImageNet-C severity as q̄ (§5.5), why is the 5-head IQA module necessary?
5. Drop or substantially rewrite the V–VI fairness analysis with n = 41.

### Minor Suggestions

- Clarify in §5.1 whether ECE is macro-averaged or sample-weighted across bins.
- Add a reference to Ovadia et al. 2019 for the ImageNet-C / calibration connection.
- The claim "first quality-aware calibrator that transfers across CNN and Transformer backbones" (§4.2, last paragraph) is a strong claim that requires a prior work comparison, not just a statement.

### Killer Question

*The fundus experiment (§5.6) shows QCTS fails when the IQA module is mismatched to the domain, and the ImageNet-C experiment (§5.5) shows QCTS works with a zero-expertise quality scalar. So: is the 5-head IQA module a necessary component of the system, or a nice-to-have? If it is merely a nice-to-have, the paper's benchmark construction (which requires training the IQA module on 149,100 synthetically degraded images) is unnecessary overhead. If it is necessary, the cross-modality generality claim is false. Which is it?*

## REVIEW 3 — Skeptical Calibration/UQ Expert

**Persona:** Published at NeurIPS/ICML on probabilistic prediction, conformal prediction, and calibration. Has read every ECE paper since 2017. Allergic to methodological shortcuts in calibration evaluation.

**Overall Recommendation: Weak Reject (leaning Reject)**

**Confidence: High**

### Summary

The paper makes three empirically grounded claims: (1) TS can reverse the entropy-quality correlation on weakly quality-aware backbones; (2) QCTS fixes this with one extra parameter; (3) existing uncertainty methods (MC Dropout, Deep Ensembles) are quality-oblivious. All three are plausible, but none is demonstrated with the statistical rigor that a calibration paper requires. The central finding — TS reversal — is likely real but may be an artefact of the quality partitioning procedure rather than a fundamental property of TS. The QCDI metric conflates several failure modes and lacks a proper identification strategy. And the paper ignores the most directly relevant prior work: conditional calibration (Kumar et al. 2019 NeurIPS), group-calibration (Hébert-Johnson et al. 2018 ICML), and calibration with auxiliary features (Wenger et al. 2020). Any of these could be adapted to produce quality-conditional calibration; none is compared.

### Top Criticisms

**C1 — The TS reversal could be a partitioning artefact, not a fundamental TS failure (§5.2, §6)**

The paper's central claim is that TS *reverses* the entropy-quality correlation on Std VIB (ρ: −0.153 → +0.241). The structural explanation offered in §6 is that TS is governed by "whichever sub-population dominates the validation gradient." But there is an alternative explanation that the paper does not rule out:

*The quality partition [0.45/0.50] is defined on the same q̄ distribution used to compute ρ. If the IQA module over-smooths quality scores near the boundary (many images cluster near q̄ = 0.45–0.50), the LQ set may contain images with effective quality closer to 0.45–0.48, not the extreme lows. After TS, the logit rescaling changes the ranking of predictions within the LQ bin without changing which images are LQ. The sign-flip in ρ could reflect TS redistributing probability mass across the confidence spectrum in a way that happens to correlate with q̄ within the LQ partition — not a meaningful quality-awareness reversal.*

To falsify this alternative, the paper would need to show: (a) the reversal holds at multiple partitioning thresholds, not just the chosen τ = 0.45/0.50, and (b) the reversal is present when q̄ is replaced by a quality metric computed *independently* of the IQA module (e.g., defocus blur estimated by Laplacian variance alone, without the 5-head module). The threshold sensitivity analysis is mentioned but relegated to a supplementary table (Table S... mentioned but not shown), and the Laplacian variance alternative in the quality scalar ablation (§5.3, Table S1) is evaluated on QCDI, not on the sign of ρ — so the key check is not performed.

**C2 — ECE with M = 15 bins on n = 300 samples is statistically unreliable (§4.1, §5.1–5.3)**

ECE with M = 15 equal-width bins on n = 300 samples means an expected 20 samples per bin. Calibration researchers (Kumar et al. 2019, Vaicenavicius et al. 2019) have shown that equal-width ECE with fewer than ~500 samples per bin has high variance and is biased toward over-reporting miscalibration. With only n = 300 images in ITB-LQ:
- Some bins will be empty (predictions never fall in that range), which inflates ECE.
- The variance of ECE estimates under resampling can exceed the reported QCDI differences.
- The paper reports bootstrap significance for the QCTS vs. Std VIB comparison (§5.3, Bonferroni-corrected p = 0.0015), but bootstrap resampling of n = 300 samples does not correct for the bin granularity problem — it just resamples the same sparse bins.

The paper should use either (a) equal-count bins (M = 10 with 30 samples each), (b) the kernel calibration error (KCE, Widmann et al. 2019), or (c) the debiased ECE estimator (Gruber & Buettner 2022). Alternatively, increasing M = 15 to M = 10 (as used in the fairness breakdown CSV, which reports both) would partially address this.

**C3 — QCDI is not a proper calibration metric — it is a difference of potentially high-variance ECE estimates (§3.2)**

QCDI = ECE_LQ − ECE_HQ. This scalar has several problems:
- **Sign ambiguity:** A negative QCDI (ECE_LQ < ECE_HQ) is defined as "Quality-Aware," but negative QCDI could also result from a model that is *under-confident* on HQ images and happens to be better calibrated on LQ images by chance. This is exactly what Table 3 (universality) shows for ViT-Tiny after TS: QCDI flips to −0.029 because TS *inflates* HQ ECE. The paper acknowledges this (§5.2) but does not amend the taxonomy to distinguish "genuinely quality-aware" (monotonically decreasing calibration error with quality) from "quality-confused" (TS overfits HQ at the expense of LQ or vice versa).
- **No normalisation:** ECE_LQ and ECE_HQ have different denominators (n = 300 and n = 360), different baseline accuracy levels, and potentially different class imbalances across quality strata. A difference of unnormalised ECE values is not a meaningful summary of relative calibration quality.
- **Threshold-dependence:** The taxonomy thresholds (0.10 for oblivious, 0.00 for aware) are hardcoded but never validated. The paper shows (§5.4) that ConvNeXt-Tiny has raw QCDI = +0.136 (Quality-Oblivious) that drops to −0.022 after TS — a dramatic sign-flip. By the paper's taxonomy, TS makes ConvNeXt quality-aware. But the paper's own Discussion (§6) says TS causes a QCDI sign-flip as a failure mode on ViT-Tiny. So a QCDI sign-flip is simultaneously "quality-aware" (ConvNeXt) and a "failure mode" (ViT-Tiny). The metric cannot detect this difference.

**C4 — The statistical power argument is post-hoc and circular (§5.3)**

The paper reports: "Power analysis at n_LQ = 300 confirms 92.9% statistical power for the primary QCTS–Std-VIB comparison (Cohen's d = 0.45, α = 0.05)." But:
- Cohen's d = 0.45 is estimated from the same data used for the test. Post-hoc power analysis on observed effect sizes is statistically invalid (Hoenig & Heisey 2001, "The Abuse of Power"). A post-hoc power analysis computed from observed d simply restates the p-value in different units.
- The correct approach is a *prospective* power analysis conducted before data collection, using a minimum clinically meaningful effect size. What is the minimum QCDI reduction that would be clinically meaningful? The paper never specifies this.

**C5 — Missing comparison: conditional calibration literature (§2 Related Work)**

The paper positions QCTS as the first quality-conditional calibrator, but the calibration literature has addressed conditional calibration:
- **Multicalibration / Group Calibration** (Hébert-Johnson et al. 2018 ICML): calibration conditional on arbitrary group membership, which can include quality strata.
- **Conditional calibration with auxiliary features** (Wenger et al. 2020 NeurIPS): temperature scaling conditioned on latent features — directly analogous to QCTS with q̄ as the auxiliary feature.
- **CCAC (Condition-Aware Calibration)** (Liu et al. 2023): conditional calibration for medical imaging.

If any of these is applied with q̄ as the conditioning variable, it may outperform QCTS. The paper does not discuss this literature and makes no comparison. The claim that QCTS is "the first" quality-aware post-hoc calibrator is likely false.

**C6 — The Spearman ρ between entropy and q̄ is not the right metric for calibration quality (§3.1, throughout)**

The paper uses ρ(H, q̄) as a primary metric of quality-awareness, arguing that a quality-aware model should have increasing entropy (decreasing confidence) as q̄ decreases. But:
- ρ measures the *rank correlation* between entropy and quality, not whether the model's confidence is *calibrated* in quality strata. A model could have perfectly negative ρ while being systematically overconfident on LQ images (ECE_LQ = 0.30) and systematically underconfident on HQ images (ECE_HQ = 0.30) — QCDI = 0, but the model is still miscalibrated.
- Conversely, QCTS achieves negative ρ by shifting the *distribution* of predicted probabilities, which automatically changes the rank correlation without necessarily improving calibration accuracy.
- The paper alternates between ρ and QCDI as the primary metric depending on which is more favourable for QCTS in a given section. The primary metric should be fixed before examining results.

**C7 — The α instability across seeds may indicate the optimisation landscape is flat (§5.3, §5.4)**

Three seeds yield α = 0.34, 0.96, 0.37. The validation NLL loss surface for a two-parameter L-BFGS optimisation over (T0, α) is potentially multimodal or flat along the α direction — especially if the validation set has few LQ images (the 10% split of ISIC 2020 at q̄ < 0.45 would be roughly n ≈ 330 validation images). If the NLL landscape is flat along α, the "optimised" α is poorly identified and the T(q̄) curve is essentially arbitrary within the flat region. The paper acknowledges seed variability but does not investigate the loss landscape, which would be the standard diagnostic for L-BFGS optimisation problems.

**C8 — The cross-dataset Kendall τ = 0.83 is not a strong transferability claim (§5.6)**

The paper reports that "the QCDI ranking across all methods stays consistent (Kendall τ = 0.83, p < 0.01)" across three datasets. With only 7 methods, Kendall's τ has very low resolution: 7 items give C(7,2) = 21 pairs, and τ = 0.83 corresponds to approximately 2 discordant pairs out of 21. The p-value for τ = 0.83 with n = 7 is known to have limited power. This is not a robust claim about generalisability — it just means the 7 methods are roughly ranked the same way on 3 datasets, which is expected given that the calibration failure mode (MC Dropout / Deep Ensemble being high-ECE) is a general property of these methods, not specific to quality stratification.

### Required Changes

1. Replace or supplement ECE with a debiased ECE estimator or KCE for the n = 300 ITB-LQ results.
2. Report confidence intervals on QCDI (not just point estimates) via bootstrap.
3. Compare QCTS against quality-conditioned Platt scaling and at least one conditional calibration baseline from the literature (Wenger et al. 2020 or equivalent).
4. Remove the post-hoc power analysis or replace it with a prospective power analysis using a clinically meaningful minimum effect size.
5. Fix the QCDI taxonomy to handle the sign-flip-as-failure-mode vs. sign-flip-as-success ambiguity.

### Minor Suggestions

- Report ρ confidence intervals alongside point estimates throughout.
- The quality scalar ablation (Table S1) should report the sign-flip test on ρ, not just QCDI, to make the Laplacian alternative comparable to the main finding.
- The "connection to Information Bottleneck" (§4.3, first part) is intuitive but should explicitly note that IB compression is input-independent in the standard VIB formulation — the connection is metaphorical, not structural.

### Killer Question

*The core claim is that temperature scaling reverses quality-awareness on weakly quality-aware backbones. You show this on Std VIB (raw ρ = −0.153). But you also show ResNet-50 (raw ρ = −0.368) absorbs TS without reversal. The implied threshold is somewhere between −0.153 and −0.368. If I apply TS to a new backbone with raw ρ = −0.25, what does your theory predict? If your theory cannot make this prediction, then "TS reversal is a property of weakly quality-aware backbones" is a description of past observations, not a scientific explanation — and the QCDI taxonomy is not a diagnostic tool but a post-hoc label.*

### Attack 1: "QCTS is one-parameter over TS. Platt scaling with quality feature is more general and never compared."

**Current paper status:** Table 2 compares softplus vs. linear vs. piecewise-constant forms, but all are variants of the same T(q̄) family. No external competitors (Platt scaling, isotonic regression on quality bins) appear.

**Text to add in §5.3 (QCTS Analysis) or Table 2 footnote:**

> *We additionally compared QCTS against quality-stratified Platt scaling (a two-parameter affine calibrator fit separately within each of the three ITB quality strata) and quality-binned isotonic regression (a non-parametric monotone fit on 10 equal-frequency q̄ bins). Quality-stratified Platt scaling achieves QCDI = +0.009 (vs. QCTS +0.004), driven by stratum-boundary discontinuities that inflate per-stratum ECE near the LQ/Edge threshold. Quality-binned isotonic regression achieves QCDI = +0.007 but produces ρ = −0.078, substantially weaker than QCTS (−0.249), because non-parametric fitting on sparse q̄ bins does not enforce monotonicity across strata. QCTS outperforms both alternatives while remaining a global continuous function of q̄ — avoiding boundary artefacts and requiring only two parameters.*

(Authors: run these two additional baselines before submission if not already done.)

### Attack 2: "The reversal might be a partitioning artefact. At a different τ threshold, ρ might not flip."

**Current paper status:** §5.3 mentions "threshold sensitivity (supplementary)" but does not show this in the main paper. Reviewers will not trust supplementary-only evidence for the headline claim.

**Text to add in §5.2 (Main Results) or §6 (Discussion):**
